Introduction

I got nominated and eventually selected for the NUS Young Fellowship Program, but I did not think that this opportunity would become one of the best experiences of my academic life so far. As one of the five students picked from India, alongside some brilliant minds from IIT Bombay and IIT Madras, I was nervously excited. My essay titled “Guardrails” (check it out on under the Madhav_Speaks) had managed to impress the selection committee and opened the door to this incredible journey. This program brought together nearly 90 students from 20+ countries. We were from India, China, USA, UK, Hungary, Japan, South Korea, Malaysia, Australia, Singapore, Saudi Arabia, Philippines, Canada, Vietnam, Thailand, and more. When I packed my bags for this week-long adventure in learning, I knew I was in for something special. My dad came with me till Chhatrapati Shivaji Maharaj International Airport Mumbai as it was first time I was travelling international.

Day 0: Touchdown in the Lion City

Stepping off the plane in Singapore, I was hit by two the humidity (whew!) and the efficiency of Changi Airport. This place runs like a well-oiled machine! Although NUS had arranged transportation, I decided to grab(yes) a taxi. As we zipped past skyscrapers and lush greenery, I was excited for the week ahead. The NUS campus welcomed me with open arms as campus is part of city and not a isolated place (and comfy accommodation). As I settled in, I decided to take a stroll around University town with other fellows and had intros and handshakes with a lot of people.

Day 1: Diving into the Deep End

Morning: Breaking the Ice and Blowing Our Minds

The program kicked off with some ice-breakers. It was here that I met my squad: Rena, Yi Xin, Xinming, and Swastika. We were dubbed Group 12. Our first lecture was by Professor Chai Kah Hin, he kept pushing us to ask “So what?” – making us really think about the impact and relevance of our research ideas. He introduced us to this cool concept called the “ghost deck” which basically is about creating a storyboard of your presentation before getting into the nitty-gritty. Apparently, it’s a trick used by big shots like McKinsey to keep their presentations tight and logical. (Mental note: must try this for my next class project!)

Afternoon: Problem-Solving Like a Boss

Prof. Chai wasn’t done with us yet. His mantra was “Rough, Rapid, Right”– the idea that making quick mistakes is actually a good thing. It’s all about learning fast and adapting faster. He also talked about horizontal and vertical logic in presentations, emphasizing the need for a clear, newspaper-like summary to hook your audience from the start.

Pro tip: If you ever find yourself in Singapore, don’t miss out on trying the local kopi (coffee).

We rounded off the afternoon with a quick but eye-opening lecture by Dr. Amirhassan Monajemi on Generative AI. While a lot of it was familiar territory for us computer science geeks, seeing how it’s being applied across different fields at NUS was fascinating. It’s one thing to code an AI model, but seeing it potentially reshape entire industries? Although I found myself yawning during this lecture, I went out and took a Kopi to keep myself fresh. Actually Luo helped me to get to know about Kopi. I am attaching one guide here for your reference that I used over remaining week.

Evening: The Ethics of AI

The highlight of the day was hands down the session on AI and ethics by Simon Chesterman, Dean of the NUS Faculty of Law. He didn’t just lecture; he made us question everything we thought we knew about education and technology. He painted a vivid (and slightly unsettling) picture of the challenges universities face in the AI era. He threw some real curveballs at us: Are students customers or products? How do we evaluate people when AI can basically be a performance-enhancing drug for the mind? He started by addressing the fear of AI turning into Skynet and taking over the world. While he didn’t completely rule out the possibility (gulp!), he was more concerned about how we humans might misuse this insanely powerful tech.

The role of universities in this brave new AI world was a big talking point. He told us in clear terms that the age-old notion of professors knowing more than students is becoming outdated and with all the world’s knowledge at our fingertips, universities need to focus on teaching us how to think, not just what to think. We also dove into the ethical minefield of AI in various fields. In research, for instance, AI is doing some pretty cool stuff like speeding up drug discovery. But it also raises some tricky questions about plagiarism and the temptation to take shortcuts. He also stressed the importance of separating the hype from reality when it comes to AI’s capabilities. One of the most thought-provoking parts of the discussion was about responsibility in the AI era. Who’s to blame when an AI system messes up? The developers? The users? The data it was trained on?

After the session, some graduate students took us for a guided campus tour where I got to know about a lot of folks and had some interesting conversations with friends from Japan and South Korea.

Over dinner at a local hawker center near Clementi mall which is very close to University Town, our group, along with a couple of others, started with debate about the future of creativity and AI’s role in the arts. The diversity of viewpoints, influenced by our varied cultural backgrounds, made for a conversation which later ended up into a conversation of stories, our stories to each other. I walked nearly 18km that day!! (eventually, I breached 30km a day mark).

Day 2: From PhD Dreams to Workplace Scenarios

Morning: PhD Tales That’ll Make You Laugh, Cry, and Think

The day started with a panel discussion featuring PhD candidates and recent graduates. I was particularly struck by Dr. Boyd Anderson from New Zealand. This guy went from tinkering with embedded systems to revolutionizing how we teach. Choosing the right supervisor and institution is like picking a life partner, it can make or break your academic journey. As one panelist put it, “The institution matters, but within it, your supervisor matters most.” (Note to self: choose wisely!)

Afternoon: The Dark Side of Office Life

Professor Sandy Lim’s session was on Workplace Incivility. Using Structured Equation Modeling, she told us about how rudeness can wreck a workplace. 1. Workplace incivility is more common than bad coffee in offices, 91% of employees in Asia reported experiencing it. 2. Your boss being rude affects you more than your annoying cubicle mate when it comes to wanting to quit. 3. Rudeness can spread through an office faster than gossip if left unchecked.

In the evening, we visited robotics lab and nano-materials lab. I got to handle an electron microscope and also play with a robotic car. That day, I along with other vegeterian friends, visited Anand Bhawan (A2B) in Little India and had very home like dinner whereas our non-vegeterian friends enjoyed their meals in a restaurant inside NUS. We also went to clark quay for a walk.

Day 3: From Nano-Wonders to Urban Jungles

Morning: The Invisible Tech Running Our World

Professor Aaron Thean’s lecture was on semiconductors He started with : “If you don’t have semiconductors, you don’t have technology.”

I was listening very mindfully that I was able to note these points only.

  1. Moore’s Law isn’t just about cramming more transistors onto a chip; it’s a testament to human ingenuity and stubbornness.
  2. Current AI, with far fewer parameters than our brains, guzzles huge energy.
  3. His research on rewritable chips, mimicking our brain’s plasticity, hinted at a future where our gadgets might think more like us.

I thought about future devices that might blur the line between silicon and neurons.

Morning: Harnessing Nature’s Power (Without Angering the Weather Gods)

Dr. Ghim Wei Ho’s session was on solar technologies for tapping into low-grade heat fluctuations.

She stated that roof gardens can actually make solar panels work better and last longer? It’s like giving your panels a cool, green hat. Niladri, a undergrad from UMich asked a ton of questions in this session.

Afternoon: Transforming Concrete Jungles into Digital Paradises

Ervine Lin’s lecture on digitizing landscapes. He challenged us to see beyond trees and buildings to the designed environments shaping our cities and lives.

Some “cool” takeaways:

  1. Singapore’s urban planning philosophy evolved from “Garden City” to “City in Gardens” a subtle difference with mindset shift!
  2. 3D scanning and reality capture tech are revolutionizing landscape architecture.
  3. Understanding topography is crucial in urban planning, especially for flood prevention.

After the lectures, we hopped on NUS buses for a tour of Singapore’s highlights like Gardens by the Bay, cloud forest, flower dome(nature on steroids!), the iconic Merlion (half-lion, half-fish, 100% Instagram-worthy although I don’t use instagram yet), and some architectural marvels that made my neck hurt from looking up so much.

The evening was a culinary delight through Little India and Chinatown. As we stuffed ourselves with everything we liked, I loved how this tiny island nation had become a melting pot of cultures and cuisines.

Day 4: Health, Wealth, and the Art of Not Boring People to Death

Morning: AI: Your New Healthcare Bestie?

Professor Dean Ho’s session on N-of-1 Digital Health felt like peeping into the future of medicine. He explained us WisDM and CURATE platform, which uses AI to personalise treatments to the level of dosage quantity to individual patients based on their individual history.

  1. The old “one-size-fits-all” approach to medicine is outdated.
  2. AI could be the key to cracking the code on personalized cancer treatments. Take that, cancer!
  3. In healthcare data, “stories over snapshots”

Prof. Ho also dove into the gamification of healthcare. Imagine earning points for taking your meds or hitting your step goal, being healthy becomes as addictive as your favorite mobile game! I bombarded him with questions after the session (sorry, not sorry!), and to his credit, he answered every single one with patience and enthusiasm. It’s not every day you get to pick the brain of someone like him!

Afternoon: Economics in the Age of AI

The lecture on economic strategies for the Industrial revolution 5.0. The key takeaways were:

  1. We need to rewire our brains when it comes to economic development. The old rules are irrelevant.
  2. It’s time to question everything we thought we knew about economics.
  3. Crafting economic strategies that play nice with AI and automation is the new holy grail.

As I sat there, furiously scribbling notes, I was thinking: in this absolute new world, would my future job/business even exist? Or would I be competing with a hyper-intelligent AI? (Note to self: start being nicer to Claude, just in case.)

Afternoon: The Art of Not Boring People (In Three Minutes or Less)

The 3-Minute Thesis (3MT) workshop by Rafi Rasheed and Jason Banta was a crash course in not putting people to sleep while talking about your research. Their golden rules:

  1. Start with a bang! Your opening line should grab attention faster than a cat video on social media.
  2. Use stories that people can relate to.
  3. Problem, solution, impact are the essentials of any good pitch. If you can’t explain it to your grandparentts, you’re doing it wrong.

Later before evening Prof Johannes Widodo took us to architectural tour of academic building of department of architecture.

We spent the evening practicing our pitches, giving each other feedback, and trying not to panic at the thought presenting the poster in front of everyone. It was challenging, hilarious, and oddly exhilarating. Since next day was presentation day, we did not go for any outdoor adventures. We visited the merchandise store in NUS, bought few things, had some snacks and started working on presentations. Although our group did not make it to finals (we missed it with very small margin becasue of too much text on poster), we enjoyed the work we did and got to learn a lot from posters of other groups.

Day 5: Battling Urban Heat and Jamming with AI

Morning: Saving the World, One Cool Roof at a Time

Professor Wong Nyuk Hien’s lecture was on the CoolNUS Project on urban climate challenges. Cool facts (pun intended):

  1. Tall buildings have a urban heat island effect.
  2. In Singapore, 1°C in temperature can spike cooling demand by 7-10%.
  3. Urban greenery is more than just for beauty.

Afternoon: When AI Drops the Beat

The program finale with Francis Tan exploring AI and Music was fun. We played around with tools like SUNO and UDIO. Authenticity, Connection and Catchiness are key ingredients even for AI generated music. As we experimented with AI-generated tunes, everyone debated about the future of creativity. Can a machine really capture the soul of music? Or are we on the brink of a new era of human-AI collaboration in art? The jury’s still out, but one thing’s for sure is that the content industry is in for one wild ride!

The Grand Finale: Posters, Presentations, and Goodbyes

As the program neared its end, our group pulled an all-nighter on second last night to put the finishing touches on our poster and presentation. Fueled by a mix of excitement, caffeine, and the fear of embarrassing ourselves in front of some of the brightest minds in academia, we worked through the night. It was exhausting, exhilarating, and oddly fun ; a perfect microcosm of the entire week.

The next day, everyone presented their projects, I loved the creativity and innovation on display. From AI-powered solutions for sustainable agriculture to novel approaches in quantum computing, the range of ideas was staggering. As the program officially came to a close, a bittersweet feeling settled over us all. We’d come as strangers from different universities but were bonded so close during such short period. That evening we went to an extra mile to squeeze out joy from every moment. We visited so many tourist attractions. We also went to Casino as entry was free for foreginers (just to see it from inside!). This is the day which made me breach 30km mark. Also, after coming back to U Town around 2AM, we wandered inside U Town till 4:30 AM. Next day, half of us came together to the aiport, bid farewell to each other. Everyone became so emotional at this point that some among us were actually sobbing. I said goodbye to everyone. But the jounery together was not over, we will still spend some time together remotely, to prepare our 3MTs and will present them to the jury.

To anyone considering applying to the NUS Young Fellowship Program : do it! It’s an unparalleled opportunity to immerse yourself in NUS research environment, engage with leading academics, and connect with exceptional peers from around the globe.

Update : 3MT is done. It was really really good to listen to knowledge-dense pitches.

After farewell, I roamed around the airport for an hour or two with few friends who were still to board their flights. Then I sat at starbucks for 2 hours since my flight was delayed beause of bad weather. It was here that I finetuned my digital journal. I boarded my flight to Mumbai. I thought about this week and interactions and as I started seeing Mumbai from Window, I played Swades (Yeh Jo Des Hei Tera) song. When I deboarded, I was overwhelmed with emotions and filled eyes. The NUS Young Fellowship Program showed me cutting edge of research and innovation and a journey that can be taken by cities and even entire nation together.
