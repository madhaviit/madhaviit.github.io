Preface — How to Use This Guide

This document is a single, self-contained reference for the Large Language Models for Engineers domain. It is organized into six Parts that mirror the syllabus tree:

  1. Preparing and Augmenting Data — where the tokens come from, how they are validated, structured, augmented, and tokenized.
  2. Designing and Building LLM Architectures — attention, multi-head attention and its variants, transformer blocks, GPT-style decoders.
  3. Adapting and Tuning Models — full fine-tuning, instruction tuning, parameter-efficient fine-tuning (LoRA, QLoRA, DoRA, IA3, prefix / prompt / P-tuning, BitFit, adapters), RLHF, DPO, IPO, KTO, RLAIF, Constitutional AI, transfer learning.
  4. Optimizing and Scaling Model Efficiency — quantization (PTQ, QAT, GPTQ, AWQ, SmoothQuant, NF4, FP8, bitsandbytes, GGUF), knowledge distillation, mixed precision, ZeRO, FSDP, tensor / pipeline / sequence / context / expert parallelism, FlashAttention, KV cache, paged attention, speculative decoding, continuous batching, vLLM, TensorRT-LLM.
  5. Controlling and Generating Outputs — beam search, top-k, top-p (nucleus), temperature, typical sampling, min-p, repetition penalty, contrastive decoding, zero-shot / few-shot prompting, CoT, Self-Consistency, Tree-of-Thoughts, ReAct, Reflexion, program-of-thought, prompt chaining, function calling, tool use.
  6. Evaluating and Interpreting Models — perplexity, BLEU, ROUGE, METEOR, BERTScore, exact match, F1, pass@k, MMLU, GSM8K, HumanEval, MT-Bench, Arena Elo, judge-LLM evaluation, attention visualization, probing classifiers, induction heads, circuits, logit lens, tuned lens, sparse autoencoders.

Each Module contains:

  • A precise definition and motivation.
  • The full mechanism / algorithm explained step by step.
  • A clean mathematical formulation.
  • Concrete worked examples with small numbers.
  • Minimal-but-complete Python / PyTorch / HuggingFace code snippets the reader could rewrite from scratch.
  • Comparison tables.
  • Practical engineering considerations: memory, latency, hyperparameter ranges, failure modes, debugging tips.
  • “Common assessment traps” sidebars where vocabulary distinctions matter.

Three appendices at the back provide a glossary, a curated reading list of canonical papers and influential blog posts, and a 50-question self-test bank.

The goal is not breadth alone but the connections: tokenization choices propagate to data preparation, to vocabulary sizing, to embedding tables, to memory at inference time, to which decoding strategies even apply. Each part repeatedly references the others.

PART 1 — Preparing and Augmenting Data

The single biggest determinant of an LLM’s quality is the data it was trained on. Compute is uniformly available; algorithms are largely public; what differs across frontier labs is the corpus. Two themes pervade modern data work:

  1. Scale with quality. The Chinchilla scaling laws (Hoffmann et al., 2022) showed that for a fixed compute budget the optimal recipe is more tokens, smaller model than the GPT-3-era intuition suggested. Modern training runs ingest trillions of tokens (LLaMA 3: ~15 T; Gemma 2; Qwen 2.5). Data engineering has become as important as model engineering.
  2. Composition. A pretraining mix is a weighted blend (web text, books, code, math, scientific papers, multilingual corpora, conversation data). The mixture controls downstream capabilities. Synthetic data — generated by stronger models or by symbolic procedures — is now a first-class component of frontier training mixes.

This Part covers two Modules:

  • Module 1. Collecting and validating data — sourcing text at scale and staying inside the law.
  • Module 2. Enhancing and structuring training inputs — multimodal preprocessing, synthetic data, and tokenization.

MODULE 1 — Collecting and Validating Data

1.1 Scrape and Aggregate Cross-Domain Text Data

Definition and motivation. “Cross-domain text data” means a deliberate mixture of sources spanning the breadth of human language and knowledge — the open web, encyclopedic prose, books, scientific papers, code, dialogue, government data, and curated mid-size collections. LLMs generalize because their training corpus did. A model fed only news articles cannot reason about code, cannot answer math questions, cannot follow legal arguments. The first job of the data team is to assemble a balanced, deduplicated, quality-filtered corpus at the trillions-of-tokens scale.

Pipeline overview (end-to-end). Assembling a pretraining corpus is a series of stages, each of which can reject most of its input.

raw sources  →  text extraction  →  language ID  →  quality filtering  →  deduplication  →  PII / toxicity filtering  →  decontamination  →  domain mixing  →  tokenization  →  shuffling + sharding  →  training-ready tokens

Every arrow above is a 5–50× data reduction stage. A typical web-scale pipeline turns 10 PB of raw HTML into a few TB of clean text.

Stage-by-stage detail.

(a) Text extraction. HTML is converted to text. Tools: trafilatura, boilerpy3, jusText, resiliparse. The goal is to strip navigation, ads, footers, scripts, while keeping the main article body. Bad extraction is the most common source of “garbage in, garbage out”.

(b) Language identification. A fastText or cld3 classifier labels each document’s language; you can keep only the languages you want, or weight them. Modern multilingual models keep 100+ languages.

(c) Quality filtering. Removes documents that are clearly low-value. Common heuristics:

  • Length thresholds (drop <50 words or >100k words).
  • Symbol-to-word ratio limits.
  • Stop-word presence (real prose has lots of common words).
  • Repetition filters (drop pages where the top n-gram covers >20% of the text).
  • Perplexity filtering with a small reference LM (drop documents the LM finds bizarre — typically machine-generated SEO spam, OCR errors, encoding glitches).
  • Classifier-based filters (e.g., FineWeb-Edu trains a classifier on “is this educational?” and applies a threshold).

(d) Deduplication. Two flavors:

  1. Exact deduplication — remove identical documents (often via MD5 / SHA hashes), and identical paragraphs / line stretches.
  2. Near-duplicate (fuzzy) deduplication — typically MinHash + LSH over shingles, or SimHash. Removes documents whose Jaccard similarity over \(k\)-gram shingles is above a threshold (commonly 0.8). Lee et al. (2022) (“Deduplicating Training Data Makes Language Models Better”) showed substantial perplexity improvements and reduced memorization from aggressive dedup. Frontier corpora are deduplicated at multiple granularities — document, paragraph, even line.

(e) PII filtering. Regex and ML-based scrubbers remove emails, phone numbers, SSNs, credit cards, IPs. Some pipelines also redact named entities like people’s addresses.

(f) Toxicity / NSFW filtering. Classifiers (e.g., Detoxify, Perspective API-style models) score documents; the highest-toxicity tail is dropped or down-weighted.

(g) Decontamination. Removes any text that appears in the evaluation benchmarks (MMLU questions, GSM8K problems, HumanEval prompts). This is non-negotiable; benchmarks leak into the web crawl and would otherwise inflate scores artificially. Methods range from exact-string substring search to n-gram overlap detection.

(h) Domain mixing. Each subset is sampled with a weight \(w_d\) chosen by the data team. Books may be upsampled, low-quality web downsampled. Mix weights are tuned experimentally — small “scaling proxy” runs at 1–7 B parameters explore the space, then the chosen mix is applied to the production model.

(i) Tokenization, shuffling, sharding. Final documents are tokenized (Section 2.3), concatenated with end-of-document tokens, shuffled, and packed into binary shards (e.g., .bin files with offsets). At training time these shards stream into a DataLoader that yields fixed-length sequences.

Mathematical view of the mixture. Let domains \(d \in \{1,\dots,D\}\) each have a deduplicated token count \(N_d\) and an assigned mixture weight \(w_d\). The expected number of times each token in domain \(d\) is seen during a \(T\)-token training run is

\[ \text{epochs}_d = \frac{T \cdot w_d}{N_d} \]

If \(\text{epochs}_d > 1\) the domain is upsampled; if \(<1\) it is downsampled. The Chinchilla recipe argues for roughly one epoch across the mixture; some recent work (Muennighoff et al. 2023) shows up to four epochs can be safe for high-quality subsets.

Worked example. Suppose your corpus has Web (1 T tokens), Books (50 B), Code (200 B), Math (10 B). You decide to train on \(T=300\) B tokens with mix weights \(\{0.5, 0.15, 0.25, 0.10\}\). Then per-domain epoch counts are:

\[ \text{Web: } \frac{0.5 \cdot 300}{1000} = 0.15, \quad \text{Books: } \frac{0.15 \cdot 300}{50} = 0.9 \] \[ \text{Code: } \frac{0.25 \cdot 300}{200} = 0.375, \quad \text{Math: } \frac{0.10 \cdot 300}{10} = 3.0 \]

Math is seen three times; Web is seen 15% of the way through. The team would either grow the math corpus, accept the repetition (often fine if quality is high), or rebalance.

Practical engineering considerations.

  • Don’t crawl yourself unless you must. The Common Crawl Foundation has been doing it for a decade; reuse the WARC files.
  • Streaming everywhere. A pipeline that materializes the full corpus to disk multiple times is too slow. Use streaming map-reduce (Spark, Ray Data, Hadoop, datatrove).
  • Idempotent stages. Each stage should be re-runnable; store outputs in immutable shards keyed by content hash.
  • Profile early. Before scaling to 10 PB, run the full pipeline on 1% of the data; measure dropout rate at each stage.
  • Provenance. For every token in the final corpus, you should be able to trace back to the source URL or document ID. Auditing, takedown requests, and decontamination demand it.

Common assessment trap. Scraping ≠ training-ready data. Raw scrape is the input; the training corpus is the output of a long filtering pipeline. The bullet point “scrape and aggregate cross-domain text data” includes everything above.

Minimal Python sketch — text extraction + language ID + length filter.

import trafilatura
import fasttext

lid = fasttext.load_model("lid.176.bin")  # language identifier

def extract_and_filter(html: str, min_words: int = 50) -> str | None:
    text = trafilatura.extract(html, favor_recall=False)
    if not text:
        return None
    if len(text.split()) < min_words:
        return None
    lang, conf = lid.predict(text.replace("\n", " ")[:512])
    if lang[0] != "__label__en" or conf[0] < 0.65:
        return None
    return text

Minimal Python sketch — MinHash deduplication.

from datasketch import MinHash, MinHashLSH

def shingles(text: str, k: int = 5) -> set[str]:
    tokens = text.split()
    return {" ".join(tokens[i:i+k]) for i in range(len(tokens) - k + 1)}

def minhash_of(text: str, num_perm: int = 128) -> MinHash:
    m = MinHash(num_perm=num_perm)
    for sh in shingles(text):
        m.update(sh.encode("utf-8"))
    return m

lsh = MinHashLSH(threshold=0.8, num_perm=128)

def add_or_skip(doc_id: str, text: str) -> bool:
    m = minhash_of(text)
    if lsh.query(m):       # near-duplicate of something already kept
        return False
    lsh.insert(doc_id, m)
    return True

1.2 Compliance with Licensing and Copyright in LLM Data Collection

Definition and motivation. LLM training corpora are dominated by text written by other people. Most of that text is copyrighted by default the moment it is fixed in tangible form. Whether you may train on it, and what rights the resulting model carries, is a legal question — but it is also an engineering question because compliance must be designed into the pipeline. Failures here have produced lawsuits (NYT v. OpenAI, Authors Guild v. OpenAI, Getty v. Stability) and forced model retractions.

The legal categories you need to recognize.

Category What it means Examples
Public domain No copyright (expired, never had one, dedicated). Pre-1928 US works, government documents, CC0-dedicated works
Permissive open license Reuse allowed with conditions (often attribution). MIT, Apache-2.0, BSD, CC-BY
Copyleft / share-alike Derivatives must carry a similar license. GPL, AGPL, CC-BY-SA
Non-commercial Commercial use forbidden. CC-BY-NC, research-only
No-derivatives Verbatim only, no modification. CC-BY-ND
All rights reserved Default for any unlicensed work. Most websites, books, news articles
Fair use / fair dealing Doctrine (US / UK / Canada) allowing limited use without permission. Quotation, criticism, parody; transformative use
Robots / TDM exceptions Some jurisdictions (EU TDM exception under DSM Directive) allow text and data mining unless rightsholders opt out. EU, Japan, Singapore
Privacy regimes (GDPR, CCPA) Personal data has separate protections orthogonal to copyright. Names, emails, location
Special data classes Health (HIPAA), children (COPPA), biometrics. Subject to specific rules

Sources of obligations to track.

  1. The site’s robots.txt — a crawler-etiquette signal; ignoring it is poor practice and increasingly cited in litigation.
  2. ai.txt / LLMs.txt — emerging conventions for sites to declare AI-training opt-outs.
  3. Terms of Service — many sites’ ToS forbid automated scraping or training.
  4. Licensing metadata — Creative Commons license tags, SPDX identifiers in source code, package metadata.
  5. Country of operation — EU TDM exception, Japan’s broad ML exception, US fair-use doctrine, UK exception. The legal posture is not uniform.

Engineering controls inside the pipeline.

  • Source whitelisting. Maintain an allowlist of source domains with vetted licenses. Anything outside the allowlist is staged for legal review or excluded.
  • License classification. Tag each document with its license at ingestion; do not strip license metadata. For code corpora, parse LICENSE, package.json, pyproject.toml, SPDX headers. For Common Crawl, use the page’s CC license tags if present.
  • Robots.txt cache. Snapshot the relevant robots.txt for each crawl; record what the policy was at the moment of the crawl.
  • PII redaction. Required under GDPR / CCPA for personal data — regardless of copyright. Mask emails, phone numbers, addresses, government IDs.
  • Opt-out registries. Companies like Spawning maintain Have I Been Trained? registries; honor takedown requests by removing documents from future runs and (where possible) by retraining.
  • Decontamination of evaluation sets. Distinct from compliance, but mentioned here because it is a similar “what should not be in the corpus?” engineering control.
  • Attribution preservation. For attribution-required licenses (CC-BY, many code licenses), keep author + URL metadata; some labs publish “Datasheet for the Dataset” disclosures with this information.

Doctrine: training as fair use. US labs commonly argue that ingesting copyrighted text to train a model is transformative and falls under fair use. Counterarguments: the model can reproduce verbatim training data (“regurgitation”); the model competes commercially with the works it was trained on; the use is not de minimis. Courts have not fully resolved this. Engineering responses to mitigate risk:

  • Memorization mitigation. Aggressive dedup reduces memorization (Lee et al., 2022). Training-data extraction attacks (Carlini et al., 2021, 2023) are the empirical measurement: if your model can be prompted to reproduce a passage verbatim, it has memorized it.
  • Output filtering. A second model or rule-based filter catches near-verbatim regurgitation in outputs.
  • “Clean room” datasets. Some labs (e.g., Adobe Firefly’s stock-photo corpus, BigCode’s permissively-licensed-only The Stack) deliberately constrain to permissive licenses to side-step the question.

The Books3 cautionary tale. Books3 was a corpus of ~196k pirated books used in The Pile and (allegedly) in early LLaMA training. After legal complaints, it was retracted from public hosting; new pretraining mixes (RedPajama, Dolma) explicitly exclude it. Engineering lesson: provenance documentation is a defensive asset.

Comparison: license classes and training implications.

License class Use for training? Conditions
Public domain / CC0 Yes None
CC-BY, MIT, Apache-2.0, BSD Yes Preserve attribution metadata; consider attributing in model card
CC-BY-SA, GPL Risky Output may inherit license obligations (debated)
CC-BY-NC Forbidden for commercial models OK for research-only
CC-BY-ND Risky Training likely creates derivative work

Operational checklist.

  1. Catalog every source with a license tag and a snapshot of its robots/ToS state at crawl time.
  2. Maintain a denylist for known opt-outs and takedowns.
  3. Run periodic memorization probes against your trained model; if a copyrighted passage can be extracted with a short prompt, consider retraining without that source.
  4. Publish a model card and dataset card disclosing categories and sizes of training data, in line with Bender et al. (2018, “Datasheets for Datasets”) and Mitchell et al. (2019, “Model Cards”).
  5. Honor takedown requests promptly; record them; remove the relevant documents before the next training run.

Common assessment trap. “Compliance” is not a single rule — it is a stack: copyright (per work), licensing (per work), site terms (per source), robots/ai.txt (per source), privacy laws (per data subject), jurisdiction (per company location). All four must clear before a document is “safe to train on” by a conservative standard.

MODULE 2 — Enhancing and Structuring Training Inputs

2.1 Preprocess and Integrate Multimodal Data for LLM Training

Definition and motivation. A multimodal model accepts inputs from more than one modality — text, images, audio, video, code, tabular, structured documents — and predicts targets in one or more modalities. “Multimodal LLM” usually means a model whose backbone is a language model, augmented to accept non-text inputs by embedding them into the same token space as text. Examples: CLIP (Radford et al., 2021), Flamingo (Alayrac et al., 2022), BLIP-2 (Li et al., 2023), LLaVA (Liu et al., 2023), GPT-4V, Gemini, Llama 3.2 Vision, Pixtral, Qwen2-VL, Whisper (audio encoder), AudioPaLM, Florence, Idefics, Molmo.

The unifying recipe. Almost all modern multimodal LLMs follow the same three-component pattern.

modality input  →  modality-specific encoder  →  projector  →  token sequence  →  shared transformer (LLM)  →  text out
  • A modality-specific encoder turns raw signal into a feature sequence (vision: ViT or ConvNeXt; audio: Whisper or wav2vec; video: spatiotemporal ViT).
  • A projector (a small MLP or linear layer, sometimes a Q-Former) maps those features into the LLM’s embedding dimension and (often) compresses the sequence length.
  • The resulting “soft” tokens are concatenated with text token embeddings and fed through the LLM as if they were text.

Image preprocessing pipeline (detailed).

  1. Decode (PNG/JPEG → tensor) and convert to RGB.
  2. Resize. Most ViTs expect a fixed input size (e.g., \(224\times224\), \(336\times336\), \(448\times448\)). Some models (Qwen2-VL, Pixtral) support native dynamic resolution by patchifying at arbitrary aspect ratios and using 2D RoPE.
  3. Normalize. Subtract the dataset mean, divide by the standard deviation (e.g., ImageNet: mean \((0.485,0.456,0.406)\), std \((0.229,0.224,0.225)\)).
  4. Patchify. Cut the image into non-overlapping patches (typically \(14\times14\) or \(16\times16\)). A \(336\times336\) image with \(14\times14\) patches yields \(24\times24=576\) patches.
  5. Encode. A ViT produces a \(576 \times d_v\) feature sequence (plus a CLS token).
  6. Project. A 2-layer MLP maps each \(d_v\)-vector to \(d_{\text{model}}\), the LLM’s hidden size. The result is a sequence of “image tokens” that look, to the LLM, like ordinary token embeddings.
  7. Pool / compress (optional). A Q-Former (BLIP-2) or perceiver resampler (Flamingo) compresses the 576 image tokens to, say, 32 or 64 — saving downstream context length.

Image–text data format. Training examples are typically chat templates with <image> placeholders:

USER:
<image>
What is happening in this picture?

ASSISTANT:
A black-and-white dog is catching a frisbee in a park.

During tokenization, the <image> placeholder is replaced by the projected image-token sequence. The text loss is computed only on the assistant response tokens (image tokens are not predicted).

Audio preprocessing pipeline.

  1. Resample to a target rate (Whisper uses 16 kHz).
  2. Compute log-mel spectrogram (e.g., 80 mel bins, 25 ms window, 10 ms hop) over 30-second chunks. Output shape: \((80, 3000)\).
  3. Encode with a transformer encoder (Whisper) → sequence of audio features.
  4. Project to LLM embedding dim and prepend / interleave with text tokens.

For audio-out models (AudioPaLM, MusicLM), the target is also a token stream — typically discrete audio tokens from a neural codec (SoundStream, Encodec).

Video preprocessing. Frames are sampled (e.g., 1–8 fps), each is patchified, and the temporal dimension is handled either by stacking frame tokens (and using a temporal positional encoding) or by a spatiotemporal ViT with 3D patches. Long videos require aggressive compression — a 1-hour video at 8 fps is 28,800 frames; even at 32 tokens per frame that is ~1 M tokens.

Tabular / structured data. Two approaches:

  1. Serialize to text (“Column A is 5, Column B is 7…”) — works because the LLM was trained on text; loses some inductive bias.
  2. Custom encoder. TabTransformer, FT-Transformer, TaBERT — train an encoder that emits embeddings consumed by the LLM.

Code as a modality. Code is technically text, but it is often handled specially: a code-aware tokenizer (often byte-level BPE trained jointly on prose + code, e.g., StarCoder, CodeLlama), file-level context windows with <fim_*> (“fill-in-the-middle”) tokens for code infilling tasks.

Alignment objectives.

  • Contrastive (CLIP-style). \(\mathcal{L}=-\log \frac{\exp(\langle f(x_i), g(y_i)\rangle/\tau)}{\sum_j \exp(\langle f(x_i), g(y_j)\rangle/\tau)}\). Trains the image encoder and text encoder to land each (image, caption) pair near each other in a shared space. Used for retrieval, zero-shot classification, and to initialize the projector that ports image features into an LLM.
  • Image captioning / VQA loss. Cross-entropy on the assistant tokens, conditioned on image features. Used in LLaVA-style supervised fine-tuning.
  • Visual instruction tuning. Chat datasets where each instruction is paired with one or more images.

Memory and compute considerations.

  • Image tokens inflate context length. A \(448\times448\) image at \(14\times14\) patches contributes 1,024 tokens before projector compression.
  • Vision encoders typically run in fp16/bf16; their KV cache contributes to GPU memory.
  • For long video, expect to need temporal sub-sampling, projection compression, and/or aggressive sequence pruning to fit context windows.

Common assessment traps.

  • “Multimodal” does not require multiple modalities at output — many models are vision-in, text-out.
  • A “vision encoder” is not the same as a “projector”; the encoder is large (ViT), the projector is a small MLP whose job is dimensional and (sometimes) sequence-length adaptation.
  • Image tokens are continuous embeddings, not discrete IDs (unlike text tokens). They never go through an embedding-lookup table inside the LLM.

2.2 Synthetic Data Generation Techniques for LLMs

Definition and motivation. Synthetic data is training (or evaluation) data generated by a model or a procedure rather than scraped from human-produced sources. It has become a cornerstone of modern LLM development for three reasons:

  1. Public web is finite and noisy. Frontier teams have already scraped most of it; further data must come from new sources or be created.
  2. Targeted capability gain. Synthetic data lets you control distribution. Want better math? Generate millions of math problems. Want a particular response style? Synthesize chats with that style.
  3. Privacy and licensing safety. Synthetic data is owned by the generator; it can substitute for sensitive real data.

Synthetic data appears in every stage of training: pretraining (rare), instruction tuning (universal), preference data (universal), evaluation (common).

Major techniques and where they apply.

Technique Where used Idea
Self-instruct (Wang et al., 2022) Instruction tuning Seed with a few human-written instructions; have an LLM generate many more, then generate responses
Evol-Instruct (WizardLM; Xu et al., 2023) Instruction tuning Take an instruction; “evolve” it to be harder/deeper using LLM rewrite operators (constraints, complications, multiple steps)
Distillation SFT / continued pretraining Use a stronger teacher LLM to produce labeled data; train a smaller student
Self-play / debate Reasoning fine-tuning Two LLM copies critique each other’s outputs; keep the wins
Bootstrapped reasoning (STaR, ReST, V-STaR) Reasoning fine-tuning Generate reasoning chains; keep only those whose final answer is correct; fine-tune on the kept set
Rejection sampling RLHF / SFT Generate \(n\) responses, keep the best by a reward model
Constitutional AI / RLAIF (Bai et al., 2022) Preference tuning Use AI feedback (a rubric + LLM judge) to label preference pairs instead of humans
Templated generation Math, code, structured Generators (SymPy for math, fuzzers for code) emit deterministic problems with verified answers
Back-translation / paraphrasing Multilingual / robustness Translate to language \(B\) and back, or paraphrase, to enlarge the dataset
Persona-driven generation Diversity Condition the generator on persona, role, demographic, locale to expand coverage
Tool-use traces Agentic fine-tuning Run an LLM in a sandbox with tools; record successful trajectories; train on them
Synthetic preferences DPO / IPO data Score \(n\) generations with a reward model; pair best vs. worst

Self-Instruct algorithm (Wang et al., 2022) — step by step.

  1. Start with a seed pool of ~175 hand-written (instruction, input, output) triples spanning many tasks.
  2. Sample 6 seeds from the pool and prompt the LLM: “Generate 8 new instructions in the style of the following examples.”
  3. Filter generated instructions: drop near-duplicates of existing ones (ROUGE-L > 0.7), drop those whose first word looks like a “classification” task already overrepresented.
  4. Generate inputs and outputs for each new instruction, again by prompting the LLM.
  5. Add the surviving (instruction, input, output) triples to the pool.
  6. Repeat until you have ~50k–100k examples.

The Stanford Alpaca dataset (Taori et al., 2023) is the canonical example: 175 seeds → 52k Alpaca instructions generated by text-davinci-003.

Evol-Instruct (WizardLM). Defines a set of “evolution operators”:

  • In-depth evolving — add constraints, deepen, concretize.
  • In-breadth evolving — generate a completely different but related instruction.

Each round, an instruction is rewritten with a randomly chosen operator. A discriminator filters degenerate evolutions (too short, off-topic, no progress). The result is a corpus that is more diverse and harder than the seed.

Rejection sampling (a.k.a. Best-of-\(n\)). Given a prompt \(x\) and a reward model \(r(x,y)\):

for prompt x in dataset:
    candidates = [policy.sample(x) for _ in range(n)]
    y* = argmax_y r(x, y)
    keep (x, y*) for SFT

Used as both a training-data generator (LLaMA 2 used it heavily for SFT data) and as a cheap inference-time alternative to RL (output the best-of-\(n\) at serving).

STaR (Self-Taught Reasoner; Zelikman et al., 2022).

  1. Generate a chain-of-thought + answer for each problem.
  2. Keep \((x, \text{cot}, y)\) tuples where \(y\) matches the gold answer.
  3. For problems the model gets wrong, rationalize: provide the model with the gold answer and ask it to generate a CoT that arrives at it; keep that.
  4. Fine-tune on the resulting (problem, CoT, answer) corpus.
  5. Iterate.

Synthetic preference data. Given a base model and a reward model:

  1. Sample \(n\) completions per prompt.
  2. Score them with the reward model.
  3. For each prompt, pick the top-1 (chosen) and bottom-1 (rejected) as a preference pair \((x, y_w, y_l)\).
  4. Train via DPO (see Section 6.1.4).

Quality-control mechanisms.

  • Diversity filters. Embed each new sample and reject if cosine similarity > threshold to anything already in the set.
  • Length filters. Drop one-line outputs or pathologically long ones.
  • Format filters. For code, require parseability. For math, require an integer / boxed answer.
  • Critic LLM. A separate LLM judges whether the response is helpful, harmless, and on-instruction; low-scoring examples are dropped.
  • Verifier-based filtering (V-STaR, math-shepherd). Use a programmatic verifier (unit tests for code, symbolic equality for math) to keep only verifiably correct examples.
  • Manual spot checks. Always sample 100 examples by hand; if 10 are bad, your pipeline has a leak.

Risks and failure modes.

  • Mode collapse. The generator imitates its own style; the dataset’s effective diversity is much smaller than its surface count.
  • Hallucination amplification. If the generator hallucinates 5% of facts and you scale to a million examples, you have 50k confidently wrong examples. Always pair with a verifier when possible.
  • Distribution drift. Synthetic data may not match the real query distribution; the model overfits to the generator’s quirks.
  • Bias inheritance. The generator’s biases (verbose, sycophantic, formatted with bullet lists) become the student’s biases.
  • License contamination. If the generator is a commercial API, its terms-of-service may forbid using the outputs to train competing models. Check before you build.
  • Reward hacking (when filtering with a reward model). The kept examples cluster around the reward model’s blind spots.

Numerical scale.

  • Alpaca: 52k instructions (~$500 of API cost in 2023).
  • Tulu-3 / OpenHermes / Capybara: 100k–1M instructions, mixed sources.
  • Phi-3 (Microsoft) trained on a mostly-synthetic curriculum of “textbook-quality” tokens (Gunasekar et al., 2023).
  • Llama 3.1 405B SFT used 25M synthetic examples (Meta’s report).

2.3 Tokenization for Language Models

Definition and motivation. Tokenization is the function that maps a raw string \(s\) to a sequence of integer IDs \(\mathbf{t} = (t_1,\dots,t_L)\) drawn from a fixed vocabulary \(V\). The reverse function (the decoder) maps IDs back to a string. Tokenizers are not learned during pretraining — they are fitted once on a sample of the training corpus, then frozen.

Why it matters:

  • Every byte of text must become a token. The vocabulary controls how many tokens the text becomes, which directly determines context length, training cost, inference cost, and KV-cache memory.
  • Bad tokenization handicaps the model on multilingual text, code, numbers, and rare words.
  • Tokenizer choice constrains everything downstream: the embedding table size, the output softmax, the maximum effective vocabulary.

Three families of tokenizers used in modern LLMs.

  1. Byte-Pair Encoding (BPE) — used by GPT-2/3/4, Llama family, Mistral, Mixtral, Falcon, Qwen, GPT-NeoX.
  2. WordPiece — used by BERT, DistilBERT, MobileBERT.
  3. Unigram language-model tokenization (and its wrapper SentencePiece) — used by T5, mT5, ALBERT, XLNet, mBART; also the SentencePiece library can be configured to train BPE.

Several refinements layer on top:

  • Byte-level BPE — operates on bytes rather than Unicode characters. Used by GPT-2/3/4, RoBERTa, Llama 3.
  • tiktoken — OpenAI’s fast byte-level BPE library; ships the vocabularies cl100k_base (GPT-3.5/4) and o200k_base (GPT-4o / o1).
  • SentencePiece — a library wrapping BPE and Unigram, supporting whitespace-as-character and language-independent tokenization.

(A) Byte-Pair Encoding (BPE).

Originally a data-compression algorithm (Gage, 1994); adopted for NMT by Sennrich, Haddow, Birch (2016) — “Neural Machine Translation of Rare Words with Subword Units.”

Training (vocabulary construction).

  1. Initialize the vocabulary as the set of all characters (or bytes) appearing in the corpus.
  2. Pre-tokenize the corpus into words (or pre-tokenizer chunks) and represent each word as a sequence of symbols, with end-of-word markers if desired (e.g., </w> for original BPE; word boundaries via leading Ġ in GPT-style byte-level BPE).
  3. Count all adjacent symbol pairs across the corpus.
  4. Merge the most frequent pair into a new symbol; add the merged symbol to the vocabulary; record the merge rule.
  5. Repeat step 3–4 until the vocabulary reaches the desired size (e.g., 32k, 50k, 100k, 200k).

Encoding (tokenizing a new string at inference).

  1. Pre-tokenize the string into words/chunks.
  2. For each chunk, split into characters/bytes.
  3. Apply the merge rules in the order they were learned until no more merges apply.
  4. Look up the integer ID of each remaining symbol.

Worked example. Suppose the corpus is low low low low low lower newer newer newer newer newer wider wider wider. With end-of-word markers:

l o w </w> : 5
l o w e r </w> : 1
n e w e r </w> : 5
w i d e r </w> : 3

Initial pair counts (some shown): (l,o)=6, (o,w)=6, (w,</w>)=5, (w,e)=1, (e,r)=9, (r,</w>)=9, …

Top pair: (e,r) with count 9. Merge → new symbol er. Update:

l o w </w> : 5
l o w er </w> : 1     (was l o w e r </w>)
n e w er </w> : 5     (was n e w e r </w>)
w i d er </w> : 3     (was w i d e r </w>)

Recount; (er, </w>) now leads with 9. Merge → er</w>. And so on. After a few iterations you accumulate er, er</w>, low, newer</w>, etc.

Encoding a new word lower (also with </w>): start l o w e r </w>. Apply merges in order: (e,r) → l o w er </w>. Then (er, </w>) → l o w er</w>. Then if you learned (l,o) → lo, etc. Final tokens depend on which merges you completed.

Byte-level BPE. Modern systems (GPT-2 onward, Llama 3) operate on bytes rather than characters. The initial vocabulary has 256 entries — one per byte — plus a few special tokens. Advantages:

  • Every Unicode string is representable; no <UNK> token ever needed.
  • A small initial alphabet means more merges spent on real subwords.
  • Multibyte characters (CJK, emoji) become 2–4 byte tokens before merging.

GPT-2 pre-tokenizes with a regex that splits punctuation, contractions, digits, and (crucially) prefixes leading whitespace into the next word so " the" becomes a single byte sequence starting with Ġ (a renamed space). This avoids learning a separate token for every word “with and without a leading space.”

Properties of BPE.

  • Deterministic. Given a tokenizer, every string has exactly one token sequence.
  • No <UNK> at byte level.
  • Greedy left-to-right. Some implementations (Llama uses the SentencePiece BPE variant) do leftmost longest match instead of strict merge-order replay. Both give similar but not identical splits.
  • Sensitive to pretokenization. Differences in whitespace and punctuation handling change tokens drastically.

(B) WordPiece.

Used by BERT, DistilBERT, ELECTRA. Conceptually similar to BPE but with a likelihood-based merge criterion.

Training.

  1. Initialize vocabulary with all characters.
  2. At each step, for each candidate merge of symbols \(a, b \to ab\), compute the score

\[ \text{score}(a, b) = \frac{\text{freq}(ab)}{\text{freq}(a) \cdot \text{freq}(b)} \]

  1. Merge the highest-scoring pair (rather than the most frequent raw pair, as in BPE).
  2. Repeat until the vocabulary size is reached.

The score \(\propto\) the pointwise mutual information; it penalizes merges where the constituents are already common (so the + space would not merge even though their joint count is huge).

Encoding. Greedy longest-match from left to right. If the longest prefix doesn’t match, fall back to a one-character token; tokens that are not word-initial are prefixed with ##. Example: "unaffable" → ["un", "##aff", "##able"].

Pros. Cleaner morphological breaks; ##-prefix makes word-boundary explicit. Cons. Cannot represent arbitrary bytes (needs an <UNK> for out-of-vocabulary characters unless using byte-level WordPiece).

(C) Unigram language-model tokenization.

Proposed by Kudo (2018), “Subword Regularization.” Used by T5, ALBERT, XLNet, mBART.

Training.

  1. Start with a large initial vocabulary (e.g., all substrings up to length \(k\) that pass a frequency cutoff).
  2. Treat tokenization probabilistically: \(p(s) = \prod_i p(t_i)\) for a segmentation \(\mathbf{t}\).
  3. Fit unigram probabilities \(p(t)\) by EM, maximizing the marginal likelihood \(\sum_\text{segmentations} \prod p(t_i)\) over the corpus.
  4. Prune. For each token \(t\), estimate the loss in likelihood if \(t\) were removed. Remove the bottom \(X\%\) of tokens by loss.
  5. Repeat 3–4 until the vocabulary reaches the target size.

Encoding. Tokenization is the Viterbi-best segmentation under the unigram model — viterbi over the dynamic-programming lattice of all segmentations.

Sampling. Because the model is probabilistic, you can sample alternative segmentations at training time as data augmentation (“subword regularization”). BPE-dropout (Provilkov et al., 2020) emulates this for BPE by randomly dropping merges with some probability \(p\).

Pros.

  • Probabilistic; multiple valid segmentations; data augmentation comes for free.
  • Often produces more linguistically coherent units.
  • Default in SentencePiece; supports a tokenization stochasticity knob.

Cons. Slightly slower to train; harder to reason about deterministically.

(D) SentencePiece.

A library (Kudo & Richardson, 2018) rather than a new algorithm. Two important properties:

  1. It treats whitespace as a regular character (commonly the Unicode lower-eighth-block ▁, U+2581). This means it does not need a language-specific pre-tokenizer — works the same on Japanese (no spaces) as on English.
  2. It supports both BPE and Unigram backends.

A SentencePiece tokenization of "Hello world" looks like ["▁Hello", "▁world"]. The ▁ is part of the token; decoding strips it back to whitespace.

Used by T5, mT5, ALBERT, XLNet, mBART, LLaMA 1/2 (BPE backend), Mistral, Mixtral, Gemma.

(E) tiktoken.

OpenAI’s fast byte-level BPE library, used by GPT-3.5, GPT-4, GPT-4o, and o1. Ships several named vocabularies:

Name Vocab size Models
r50k_base 50,257 GPT-2, GPT-3 davinci/curie/babbage/ada
p50k_base 50,281 code-davinci, text-davinci-002/003
cl100k_base 100,277 GPT-3.5-turbo, GPT-4, text-embedding-ada-002
o200k_base 200,019 GPT-4o, o1, GPT-4o-mini

Larger vocabularies tokenize natural language and code into fewer tokens — meaning lower API costs and longer effective contexts. o200k_base typically yields 10–15% fewer tokens than cl100k_base on English prose, and even more on code and non-English text.

Compute / cost implication. Going from a 32k to a 200k vocab adds embeddings \((200{,}000-32{,}000) \cdot d_\text{model}\) parameters, plus the same in the output head if untied. For \(d_\text{model}=4096\), that is ~688 M extra parameters. Frontier labs accept that cost because it pays for itself in tokenization efficiency.

Worked numeric example — vocab size, embedding memory, sequence cost.

Suppose a model has hidden size \(d=4096\) and you choose:

  • Vocab 32,000: embedding params \(32\text{k}\cdot 4096 = 131\) M; output head (if untied) +131 M.
  • Vocab 128,256 (Llama 3): \(\approx 525\) M; if untied, +525 M.
  • Vocab 200,019 (o200k_base): \(\approx 819\) M; +819 M if untied.

Tokenization efficiency on a 1,000-character English passage:

  • 32k BPE (e.g., Llama 2): ~250 tokens.
  • cl100k_base: ~225 tokens.
  • o200k_base: ~200 tokens.

Compute per training step scales with tokens; over a 15 T-token run, a 10% reduction in tokens-per-character is genuine money.

Numbers, code, and the “tokenization tax”.

  • Numbers. Most BPE/WordPiece tokenizers split numbers into digit chunks; "1729" may become ["172", "9"] or ["1", "7", "2", "9"]. Some models force digit-level tokenization to improve arithmetic (Llama, PaLM 2 with digit splitting).
  • Code. Tokenization affects code drastically — indentation, brace handling, identifier splits. Code-trained tokenizers (CodeLlama, StarCoder) have merges biased toward common code patterns (def, return, ).
  • Whitespace. A tokenizer that doesn’t fold leading whitespace into the next word will double its vocabulary for common words.
  • Multilingual. Languages without spaces (Chinese, Japanese, Thai) require byte-level or SentencePiece-style handling; otherwise the vocabulary explodes.

Common assessment traps.

  • BPE vs. WordPiece vs. Unigram — the merge criterion is the distinguishing characteristic (frequency vs. likelihood-score vs. EM-pruned).
  • SentencePiece is a library, not an algorithm. It can run BPE or Unigram inside.
  • Byte-level BPE has no <UNK> token. This is a frequent multiple-choice gotcha.
  • WordPiece’s ## marks continuation, not start. ["un", "##aff", "##able"] is one word; ["aff"] alone would be a standalone word.

Design checklist for choosing a tokenizer.

  1. Coverage: byte-level if you want any string to be representable.
  2. Domain: include code, math, and target languages in the training data.
  3. Vocab size: 32k is comfortable for English-only; 100k–256k for multilingual + code.
  4. Special tokens: BOS/EOS, padding, role tokens for chat (<|system|>, <|user|>, <|assistant|>), tool tokens, <image> placeholders for multimodal.
  5. Numbers and digits: consider explicit digit splitting if the model must do arithmetic.
  6. Reproducibility: ship the tokenizer file alongside model weights; never silently switch.

MODULE 3 — Implementing Attention Mechanisms

3.1 Self-Attention in LLM Architectures

Definition and motivation. Self-attention is a function that maps a sequence of vectors \(X \in \mathbb{R}^{L \times d}\) to another sequence \(Y \in \mathbb{R}^{L \times d}\) of the same length, where each output vector \(Y_i\) is a weighted sum of all input vectors, with weights learned dynamically as a function of the inputs themselves. The mechanism allows every position in the sequence to attend to (gather information from) every other position, in \(O(1)\) depth — replacing the \(O(L)\) recurrence of RNNs and the \(O(\log L)\) depth of convolutions with stacking.

The defining innovation is that the mixing weights are content-dependent: \(y_i\)’s weighting of \(x_j\) depends on a learned similarity between \(x_i\) and \(x_j\), not on a fixed positional pattern.

Full derivation — scaled dot-product attention.

Given an input sequence \(X \in \mathbb{R}^{L \times d_\text{model}}\):

  1. Project to three matrices — Queries, Keys, Values:

\[ Q = X W^Q, \quad K = X W^K, \quad V = X W^V \]

where \(W^Q, W^K \in \mathbb{R}^{d_\text{model} \times d_k}\) and \(W^V \in \mathbb{R}^{d_\text{model} \times d_v}\).

  1. Compute attention scores as scaled dot products:

\[ S = \frac{Q K^\top}{\sqrt{d_k}} \in \mathbb{R}^{L \times L} \]

The \(\sqrt{d_k}\) scaling keeps the variance of the dot products around \(1\) regardless of \(d_k\) — without it, softmax saturates and gradients vanish for large \(d_k\).

  1. Apply a mask if needed (causal mask for decoder-only LLMs; padding mask for variable-length batches):

\[ S_\text{masked}[i, j] = \begin{cases} S[i, j] & \text{if allowed} \\ -\infty & \text{otherwise} \end{cases} \]

  1. Normalize each row with softmax to obtain the attention weights:

\[ A_{ij} = \frac{\exp(S_\text{masked}[i, j])}{\sum_{k=1}^{L} \exp(S_\text{masked}[i, k])} \]

  1. Compute the output as the weighted average of values:

\[ Y = A V \in \mathbb{R}^{L \times d_v} \]

In one line:

\[ \boxed{\;\operatorname{Attention}(Q,K,V) = \operatorname{softmax}\!\left(\frac{QK^\top}{\sqrt{d_k}} + M\right) V\;} \tag{1}\]

where \(M\) is the additive mask (0 where attention is allowed, \(-\infty\) where it is forbidden).

Why this formula works — intuition.

  • \(Q\) asks a question about the current token; \(K\) advertises what each token has to offer; \(V\) is the content delivered if the question matches the advertisement.
  • \(QK^\top\) is the cosine-similarity-like compatibility matrix.
  • Softmax converts compatibilities to a probability distribution; each row sums to 1.
  • \(AV\) is the weighted retrieval of values according to those probabilities.

Worked \(2\times2\) numeric example. Let \(X = \begin{pmatrix}1 & 0\\ 0 & 1\end{pmatrix}\) (two tokens, \(d_\text{model}=2\)). Let \(W^Q = W^K = W^V = I_2\). Then \(Q=K=V=X\). Compute:

\[ QK^\top = \begin{pmatrix}1\cdot 1 + 0\cdot 0 & 1\cdot 0 + 0\cdot 1\\ 0\cdot 1 + 1\cdot 0 & 0\cdot 0 + 1\cdot 1\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}1 & 0\\ 0 & 1\end{pmatrix} \]

With \(d_k=2\), \(\sqrt{d_k}\approx 1.41\):

\[ S = \begin{pmatrix}0.707 & 0\\ 0 & 0.707\end{pmatrix} \]

Apply row-wise softmax. Row 0: \(\frac{e^{0.707}}{e^{0.707}+e^0} \approx \frac{2.028}{2.028+1} \approx 0.670\); off-diagonal \(\approx 0.330\). By symmetry, Row 1 is the mirror.

\[ A \approx \begin{pmatrix}0.670 & 0.330\\ 0.330 & 0.670\end{pmatrix} \]

\[ Y = AV = AX = \begin{pmatrix}0.670 & 0.330\\ 0.330 & 0.670\end{pmatrix} \]

Each output token is a blend dominated by itself (0.67) with a contribution from the other (0.33).

Causal masking (decoder-only LLMs). GPT-style models must not look at future tokens (otherwise next-token prediction is trivial). The mask is a lower-triangular matrix:

\[ M[i,j] = \begin{cases} 0 & j \le i \\ -\infty & j > i \end{cases} \]

After softmax, attention weights above the diagonal are zero.

Padding masking (variable-length batches). Sequences of different lengths are right-padded. The mask sets attention weights from any position to a padding position to zero.

Cross-attention vs self-attention.

  • Self-attention: \(Q, K, V\) all come from the same sequence.
  • Cross-attention: \(Q\) comes from one sequence (e.g., decoder), \(K, V\) from another (e.g., encoder). This is how the encoder-decoder transformer (T5, BART, original “Attention is All You Need”) wires the decoder to the encoder.

Complexity.

  • Time: \(O(L^2 d)\). The \(QK^\top\) product is \(L\times L \cdot d\); the \(AV\) product is \(L\times L \cdot d\).
  • Memory: \(O(L^2)\) for the attention matrix.
  • Implication: Naïve attention is the bottleneck for long contexts. FlashAttention (Section 3.2) recomputes attention without ever materializing the \(L\times L\) matrix; sparse / sliding-window / linear attention reduce the asymptotic cost.

KV cache (only at inference). During autoregressive decoding the model emits one token at a time. Without caching, generating \(L\) tokens would re-attend over the whole prefix at each step — \(O(L^3)\). Instead, we cache \(K, V\) from prior steps; each new step appends one new \(k, v\) row and the attention reads from the cache. Cost per generated token drops from \(O(L^2 d)\) to \(O(L d)\).

KV-cache memory per token per layer per head:

\[ \text{bytes per token} = 2 \cdot n_\text{layers} \cdot n_\text{heads} \cdot d_\text{head} \cdot \text{precision\_bytes} \]

For Llama-3-8B (32 layers, 32 heads, \(d_\text{head}=128\)) in FP16:

\[ 2 \cdot 32 \cdot 32 \cdot 128 \cdot 2 = 524{,}288 \text{ bytes} \approx 0.5 \text{ MB/token} \]

A 32k-token prefix consumes ~16 GB of KV cache — often the dominant memory footprint at inference time. This motivates MQA, GQA, paged attention, and KV-cache quantization (later).

Minimal PyTorch implementation.

import torch
import torch.nn as nn
import torch.nn.functional as F

class SelfAttention(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self, d_model: int, d_head: int, causal: bool = True):
        super().__init__()
        self.d_head = d_head
        self.causal = causal
        self.W_q = nn.Linear(d_model, d_head, bias=False)
        self.W_k = nn.Linear(d_model, d_head, bias=False)
        self.W_v = nn.Linear(d_model, d_head, bias=False)

    def forward(self, x):                              # x: (B, L, d_model)
        Q = self.W_q(x)                                # (B, L, d_head)
        K = self.W_k(x)                                # (B, L, d_head)
        V = self.W_v(x)                                # (B, L, d_head)
        scores = Q @ K.transpose(-2, -1) / self.d_head**0.5  # (B, L, L)
        if self.causal:
            L = x.shape[1]
            mask = torch.triu(torch.ones(L, L, device=x.device), diagonal=1).bool()
            scores = scores.masked_fill(mask, float("-inf"))
        A = F.softmax(scores, dim=-1)                  # (B, L, L)
        return A @ V                                   # (B, L, d_head)

Common assessment traps.

  • Causal vs bidirectional attention. BERT (encoder-only) uses bidirectional self-attention. GPT (decoder-only) uses causal masking. T5 uses bidirectional in the encoder and causal in the decoder.
  • The \(\sqrt{d_k}\) scaling is in the denominator of the dot product — not the value matrix.
  • The attention matrix is \(L\times L\) — not \(L\times d\). Confusing these is a classic mistake.
  • “Self-attention” and “scaled dot-product attention” are not synonyms. The first names the structural pattern (Q, K, V all from the same source); the second names the specific scoring function (dot product / \(\sqrt{d_k}\), then softmax).

3.2 Multi-Head Attention and Its Variants

Why multi-head? A single attention head averages many possible “queries” into one weighting pattern. Multi-head attention (MHA) runs \(h\) attention operations in parallel — each with its own learned projections — then concatenates and projects the result back. Each head can specialize: one tracks subject-verb agreement, another resolves coreference, another attends to syntactic parents, etc. The capacity to attend differently in different subspaces is a key driver of transformer quality.

Multi-Head Attention — the formula.

Given \(h\) heads with per-head dimension \(d_\text{head} = d_\text{model}/h\):

\[ \operatorname{head}_i = \operatorname{Attention}(X W^Q_i,\ X W^K_i,\ X W^V_i) \]

\[ \operatorname{MHA}(X) = \operatorname{Concat}(\operatorname{head}_1,\dots,\operatorname{head}_h)\, W^O \]

where \(W^Q_i, W^K_i, W^V_i \in \mathbb{R}^{d_\text{model}\times d_\text{head}}\) and \(W^O \in \mathbb{R}^{(h\cdot d_\text{head})\times d_\text{model}}\).

In practice the per-head projections are stacked into single linear layers \(W^Q \in \mathbb{R}^{d_\text{model}\times (h\cdot d_\text{head})}\) and reshaped:

Q = self.W_q(x).reshape(B, L, h, d_head).transpose(1, 2)   # (B, h, L, d_head)
K = self.W_k(x).reshape(B, L, h, d_head).transpose(1, 2)
V = self.W_v(x).reshape(B, L, h, d_head).transpose(1, 2)
scores = (Q @ K.transpose(-2, -1)) / d_head**0.5            # (B, h, L, L)
# (apply masks)
out = (scores.softmax(-1) @ V).transpose(1, 2).reshape(B, L, h * d_head)
out = self.W_o(out)

Sizing conventions. GPT-2 used \(d_\text{model}=768, h=12, d_\text{head}=64\). Llama-3-8B uses \(d_\text{model}=4096, h=32, d_\text{head}=128\). Llama-3-70B uses \(d_\text{model}=8192, h=64, d_\text{head}=128\). Note that \(d_\text{head}\) is almost always 64 or 128 — small enough that the per-head attention fits comfortably in shared memory on a GPU.

Variant 1: Multi-Query Attention (MQA). Proposed by Shazeer (2019), “Fast Transformer Decoding: One Write-Head is All You Need.”

  • All heads share one \(K\) and one \(V\), while each head keeps its own \(Q\).
  • Reduces KV-cache memory by a factor of \(h\).
  • Slight quality drop (usually a few BLEU/perplexity points at the same size).

\[ \operatorname{head}_i = \operatorname{Attention}(X W^Q_i,\ X W^K,\ X W^V) \]

Used by PaLM (Chowdhery et al., 2022), Falcon, StarCoder.

Variant 2: Grouped-Query Attention (GQA). Ainslie et al. (2023), “GQA: Training Generalized Multi-Query Transformer Models from Multi-Head Checkpoints.”

  • An interpolation between MHA and MQA: split heads into \(g\) groups; all heads in a group share one \(K, V\).
  • \(g=h\) → MHA; \(g=1\) → MQA.
  • Llama 2 70B / Llama 3 / Mistral / Mixtral / Qwen use GQA, typically with \(g=8\).

Memory savings without the quality cost of MQA. The KV cache is \(h/g\) times smaller than MHA.

Variant 3: Sliding-Window Attention. Used by Longformer (Beltagy et al., 2020), Mistral 7B (Jiang et al., 2023).

  • Each position attends only to a window of \(w\) tokens to its left (and right, for encoder).
  • Complexity drops from \(O(L^2)\) to \(O(L w)\).
  • A stack of \(n\) sliding-window layers has an effective receptive field of \(n \cdot w\) tokens, similar to convolutional dilation.

Mistral 7B used \(w=4096\) in early versions but later removed sliding-window attention in v0.2; the technique remains useful for very long contexts (Mistral Codestral Mamba, Mistral Nemo, Phi-3-Long-Window).

Variant 4: FlashAttention. Dao et al. (2022), FlashAttention; Dao (2023), FlashAttention-2; Dao et al. (2024), FlashAttention-3.

  • Not a different attention function but a different implementation of exact scaled dot-product attention.
  • Key insight: the \(L\times L\) attention matrix is never materialized in HBM (GPU global memory). Instead, the computation is tiled: load tiles of \(Q, K, V\) into SRAM, compute the softmax and the weighted sum incrementally using a numerically stable online softmax, write only the \(L\times d\) output.
  • Asymptotically the same FLOPs and memory \(O(L^2)\), but in practice 2–10× faster and uses \(O(L)\) memory because the \(L\times L\) matrix never exists in HBM.
  • FlashAttention-2 added work partitioning across warps; FlashAttention-3 added Hopper-specific FP8 and asynchronous TMA loads, hitting >700 TFLOPS on H100.

Engineering implication: use FlashAttention (via torch.nn.functional.scaled_dot_product_attention, flash-attn, or vendor-provided kernels). It is the single biggest free speedup for long-context training and inference.

Variant 5: Sparse Attention. Generalize sliding-window. Each query attends to a predetermined sparse set of keys (strided, dilated, random, BigBird’s “global tokens”). Reduces \(O(L^2)\) to \(O(L \sqrt{L})\) or \(O(L \log L)\) at the cost of approximation quality. Used in Sparse Transformers (Child et al., 2019), Longformer, BigBird (Zaheer et al., 2020), GPT-3’s sparse layers.

Variant 6: Linear Attention. Reorder the computation:

Standard: \(\operatorname{softmax}(QK^\top) V\) — \(L\times L\) matrix.

If we approximate softmax with a feature map \(\phi(\cdot)\) such that \(\operatorname{sim}(q,k)\approx \phi(q)^\top \phi(k)\), attention becomes:

\[ Y_i = \frac{\phi(q_i)^\top \sum_j \phi(k_j) v_j^\top}{\phi(q_i)^\top \sum_j \phi(k_j)} \]

The sums \(\sum_j \phi(k_j) v_j^\top\) and \(\sum_j \phi(k_j)\) are computed once and shared — \(O(L d^2)\) instead of \(O(L^2 d)\). Variants: Linformer (project keys to a smaller subspace), Performer (random Fourier features), RWKV (recurrent / linear hybrid), Mamba (selective state space).

In practice exact attention with FlashAttention has dominated linear attention for context lengths < 100k. Beyond that, SSM-based architectures (Mamba, RWKV) re-enter the conversation.

Positional-encoding variants — ALiBi and RoPE.

Attention is order-invariant by itself; the model only knows position because it is told. The classical recipe (original Vaswani) added sinusoidal positional embeddings to token embeddings:

\[ \text{PE}(\text{pos}, 2i) = \sin\!\left(\frac{\text{pos}}{10000^{2i/d}}\right),\ \text{PE}(\text{pos}, 2i+1)=\cos\!\left(\frac{\text{pos}}{10000^{2i/d}}\right) \]

Modern decoder-only LLMs use two newer schemes.

Rotary Positional Encoding (RoPE). Su et al. (2021), “RoFormer: Enhanced Transformer with Rotary Position Embedding.”

  • Rotate the query/key vectors by an angle proportional to position. For dimension pair \((2i, 2i+1)\) at position \(m\):

\[ \begin{pmatrix} q'_{m,2i}\\ q'_{m,2i+1} \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} \cos m\theta_i & -\sin m\theta_i\\ \sin m\theta_i & \cos m\theta_i \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} q_{m,2i}\\ q_{m,2i+1} \end{pmatrix} \]

with \(\theta_i = 10000^{-2i/d_\text{head}}\).

  • Key property: \(\langle q'_m, k'_n \rangle\) depends only on \(m-n\), so the relative position is encoded in the dot product.
  • Extrapolation friendly with techniques: NTK-aware scaling (extend \(\theta\)), Linear interpolation of positions (Chen et al., 2023’s “Position Interpolation”), YaRN (Peng et al., 2023) — all enable a model trained at 4k context to operate at 32k–128k.

Used by GPT-J, GPT-NeoX, Llama 1/2/3, Mistral, Qwen, Gemma, PaLM 2.

ALiBi (Attention with Linear Biases). Press et al. (2021), “Train Short, Test Long.”

  • Add a distance penalty to the attention scores, with a per-head slope:

\[ S[i,j] \leftarrow S[i,j] - m_h \cdot |i - j| \]

  • No positional embeddings at all; positional information is injected directly into attention logits.
  • Slopes \(m_h\) are a fixed geometric sequence per head.
  • Extrapolates to far longer contexts than train.

Used by BLOOM, MPT, Falcon.

Common assessment traps.

  • MQA vs GQA. MQA = 1 shared K/V; GQA = \(g\) groups of shared K/V; MHA = all heads independent.
  • FlashAttention is exact. It is not an approximation; the asymptotic FLOPs and outputs are identical to naïve attention.
  • RoPE is applied to Q and K, not V. The rotation enters the dot product; values are unchanged.
  • ALiBi has no embedding to learn; it is purely an attention-score bias.
  • Sliding-window does not reduce KV cache for the most-recent window; it just bounds how far back attention looks.

MODULE 4 — Customizing Transformer Architectures

4.1 Design Custom Transformer Models for Specific LLM Applications

The transformer is a kit, not a single design. Choices fall into five clusters.

(1) Backbone topology.

Family Encoder Decoder Cross-attention Examples
Encoder-only yes — — BERT, RoBERTa, DeBERTa, ELECTRA
Decoder-only — yes (causal) — GPT-2/3/4, Llama, Mistral, Mixtral, Falcon, Qwen, Gemma
Encoder-decoder yes yes encoder → decoder T5, BART, mT5, FLAN-T5, mBART, NLLB
Prefix-LM — yes (causal with bidirectional prefix) — UL2, PaLM (some), GLM
Mixture-of-Experts either either either Mixtral, Switch Transformer, GLaM, DeepSeek-V2/V3
  • Encoder-only models are bidirectional and great for understanding tasks (classification, NER, semantic search). Pretrained with masked language modeling (MLM).
  • Decoder-only models are unidirectional and great for generation. Pretrained with next-token prediction. Dominant for modern LLMs.
  • Encoder-decoder models combine both. Good for tasks with a clear input → output mapping (translation, summarization). Pretrained with span-masked denoising (T5) or other seq2seq objectives.
  • Prefix-LM treats a prompt as bidirectional and the generation as causal — a useful in-between.
  • MoE swaps the dense FFN with a sparse routed mixture; capacity scales while FLOPs per token stay roughly constant.

(2) Normalization.

A single transformer block applies normalization either before or after the sub-layer:

  • Post-norm (original “Attention is All You Need”):
y = LN(x + Sublayer(x))
  • Pre-norm (modern default):
y = x + Sublayer(LN(x))

Pre-norm trains more stably at scale because it gives a clean residual path; post-norm tends to require careful warmup. Almost every modern LLM uses pre-norm.

LayerNorm (Ba et al., 2016):

\[ \operatorname{LN}(x) = \gamma \odot \frac{x - \mu}{\sqrt{\sigma^2 + \epsilon}} + \beta, \quad \mu = \frac{1}{d}\sum_i x_i,\ \sigma^2 = \frac{1}{d}\sum_i (x_i - \mu)^2 \]

RMSNorm (Zhang & Sennrich, 2019):

\[ \operatorname{RMSNorm}(x) = \gamma \odot \frac{x}{\sqrt{\frac{1}{d}\sum_i x_i^2 + \epsilon}} \]

RMSNorm omits the mean subtraction and bias — fewer FLOPs, same training stability. Used by Llama, Mistral, Qwen, Gemma, PaLM.

(3) Activation in the FFN.

The position-wise feed-forward sublayer is two linear layers with a nonlinearity in between:

\[ \operatorname{FFN}(x) = W_2\, \sigma(W_1 x + b_1) + b_2 \]

Common choices for \(\sigma\):

  • ReLU (original): \(\max(0, x)\). Used in classic transformers; cheap; can have dead neurons.
  • GELU (Hendrycks & Gimpel, 2016): \(x\Phi(x)\) where \(\Phi\) is the standard normal CDF. Approximation: \(0.5 x(1 + \tanh(\sqrt{2/\pi}(x + 0.044715 x^3)))\). Smoother; used by BERT, GPT-2/3.
  • SwiGLU (Shazeer 2020, “GLU Variants”): \(\operatorname{Swish}(W_1 x)\odot W_3 x\), then \(W_2\). Two input projections instead of one; gating multiplies. Used by Llama, Mistral, PaLM, Qwen, Gemma.
  • GeGLU: same idea but with GELU instead of Swish. Used by T5 v1.1.

Gated activations (SwiGLU / GeGLU) consistently outperform vanilla MLP at the same compute, although they have 1.5× the parameters per FFN layer.

(4) Positional encoding.

Covered in Module 3:

  • Absolute sinusoidal (original).
  • Learned absolute (GPT-2).
  • Relative (T5).
  • ALiBi (BLOOM, MPT, Falcon).
  • RoPE (Llama, Mistral, Qwen, Gemma).

Modern decoder-only LLMs almost universally use RoPE with various scaling schemes for long contexts.

(5) Mixture-of-Experts (MoE).

Replace the FFN with \(E\) “expert” FFNs and a gating network that selects (or weighted-combines) the top-\(k\) experts per token. The number of active parameters per token is roughly \(1/E\) × the total parameters, while the model can still draw on the total parameter count.

Top-\(k\) routing. A small linear router produces logits over experts; softmax → take top-\(k\) experts; route the token to those experts; combine outputs via the router weights.

gate_logits = x @ W_gate           # (B, L, E)
topk_logits, topk_idx = gate_logits.topk(k, dim=-1)
gate = softmax(topk_logits, -1)    # (B, L, k)
out = sum_{j=1..k} gate[:,:,j] * Expert[topk_idx[:,:,j]](x)

Key designs:

  • Switch Transformer (Fedus et al., 2021): \(k=1\), very wide. Reaches trillions of parameters.
  • GLaM (Du et al., 2022): \(k=2\), decoder-only.
  • Mixtral 8x7B (Jiang et al., 2024): \(k=2\) out of 8 experts; 13B active parameters from 47B total.
  • DeepSeek-V2 / V3: 60+ experts per layer, fine-grained routing, shared experts; ~21B active out of 671B (V3).

Load balancing loss. Without auxiliary losses, the router collapses to a few experts. Add an auxiliary loss that encourages uniform expert utilization:

\[ \mathcal{L}_\text{aux} = \alpha \cdot E \cdot \sum_{e=1}^E f_e \cdot P_e \]

where \(f_e\) is the fraction of tokens routed to expert \(e\) and \(P_e\) is the mean router weight on that expert.

Engineering complexity. MoE adds inter-device communication (token routing across GPUs), capacity constraints (each expert can only handle so many tokens per batch), and load imbalance — but the throughput per FLOP wins are substantial at sufficient scale.

Sizing the model. The dominant levers, with Chinchilla-style scaling laws:

  • \(N\) = number of parameters.
  • \(D\) = number of training tokens.
  • \(C\) ≈ \(6 N D\) FLOPs of compute (a useful rule of thumb).

Hoffmann et al. (2022) found that, for compute-optimal training, \(N\) and \(D\) should scale roughly equally with \(C\). Their compute-optimal predictions: a 10B-parameter model deserves ~200B tokens; a 70B model deserves ~1.4T tokens. Later work (Llama 3) trained substantially “past Chinchilla” — Meta trained 70B on 15T tokens, far above the compute-optimal ratio, because inference cost rewards small models trained long.

Putting it together — a single transformer block (modern decoder-only).

import torch, torch.nn as nn, torch.nn.functional as F

class RMSNorm(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self, d, eps=1e-6):
        super().__init__()
        self.g = nn.Parameter(torch.ones(d))
        self.eps = eps
    def forward(self, x):
        r = x.float().pow(2).mean(-1, keepdim=True).add(self.eps).rsqrt()
        return (x * r).type_as(x) * self.g

class SwiGLU(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self, d, hidden):
        super().__init__()
        self.W1 = nn.Linear(d, hidden, bias=False)
        self.W3 = nn.Linear(d, hidden, bias=False)
        self.W2 = nn.Linear(hidden, d, bias=False)
    def forward(self, x):
        return self.W2(F.silu(self.W1(x)) * self.W3(x))

class Block(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self, d, n_head, hidden):
        super().__init__()
        self.norm_attn = RMSNorm(d)
        self.attn = MHA(d, n_head)        # see Module 3 for details (with RoPE)
        self.norm_ffn  = RMSNorm(d)
        self.ffn  = SwiGLU(d, hidden)
    def forward(self, x):
        x = x + self.attn(self.norm_attn(x))      # pre-norm + residual
        x = x + self.ffn(self.norm_ffn(x))
        return x

Stack 32 of these and you have the Llama-3-8B trunk.

Common assessment traps.

  • Encoder-only vs decoder-only vs encoder-decoder. Know which is bidirectional, which is causal, which has cross-attention.
  • Pre-norm vs post-norm. Pre-norm wraps the input of the sublayer; post-norm wraps the output of x + Sublayer(x).
  • RMSNorm has only a scale parameter (no bias, no mean subtraction).
  • SwiGLU has two input projections (\(W_1, W_3\)) plus an output projection (\(W_2\)), so the FFN parameter count is \(\frac{3}{2}\times\) a standard MLP at the same hidden size. Many LLM configs compensate by reducing the hidden ratio from \(4d\) to \(\frac{8}{3}d\) to keep parameter parity.

4.2 Implement GPT-Style Architectures for Generative Tasks

Definition and motivation. A GPT-style model is a decoder-only autoregressive language model: it predicts each token from the prefix to its left. The architecture is just a stack of pre-norm transformer blocks (causal self-attention + FFN), with a token embedding at the input, a positional encoding (or RoPE), and a language-model head that projects the final hidden states to vocabulary logits.

The full forward pass.

input ids  [t_0, t_1, …, t_{L-1}]
                │
                │ token embedding (lookup)
                ▼
hidden states (B, L, d)
                │
                │ (+ positional info — sinusoidal/learned, or RoPE inside attention)
                ▼
       ┌──────── × N blocks ─────────┐
       │ pre-norm + causal MHA + residual│
       │ pre-norm + FFN/SwiGLU + residual │
       └──────────────────────────────────┘
                │
                │ final RMSNorm
                ▼
hidden states (B, L, d)
                │
                │ LM head (linear, often weight-tied to embedding)
                ▼
logits (B, L, V)
                │
                │ next-token cross-entropy loss vs shifted inputs
                ▼

Weight tying. GPT-2 (and many modern LLMs) tie the input embedding matrix \(E \in \mathbb{R}^{V\times d}\) with the LM head \(W_\text{lm} = E^\top\). Saves \(V\cdot d\) parameters and improves generalization. Llama 3 and Gemma 2 untie — at very large vocabularies the extra capacity helps.

Loss. Standard next-token prediction, cross-entropy over the vocabulary, with labels shifted left by one:

\[ \mathcal{L} = -\frac{1}{L-1}\sum_{i=1}^{L-1} \log p_\theta(t_i \mid t_{<i}) \]

A pretraining run averages this loss over hundreds of billions of tokens.

Inference (generation). Given a prompt, the model produces logits at each position. The logits at the last position are used to sample the next token (Module 9 for decoding strategies). Append the sampled token, advance the KV cache by one step, repeat.

Minimal complete GPT (≈100 lines).

import torch, torch.nn as nn, torch.nn.functional as F

class CausalSelfAttention(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self, d, n_head):
        super().__init__()
        self.n_head, self.d_head = n_head, d // n_head
        self.qkv = nn.Linear(d, 3 * d, bias=False)
        self.proj = nn.Linear(d, d, bias=False)

    def forward(self, x):
        B, L, D = x.shape
        qkv = self.qkv(x).reshape(B, L, 3, self.n_head, self.d_head)
        q, k, v = qkv.unbind(dim=2)        # each (B, L, h, d_h)
        q, k, v = (t.transpose(1, 2) for t in (q, k, v))   # (B, h, L, d_h)
        # PyTorch 2 ships a fused, FlashAttention-compatible kernel:
        y = F.scaled_dot_product_attention(q, k, v, is_causal=True)   # (B, h, L, d_h)
        y = y.transpose(1, 2).contiguous().reshape(B, L, D)
        return self.proj(y)

class Block(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self, d, n_head, mlp_ratio=4):
        super().__init__()
        self.ln1 = nn.LayerNorm(d)
        self.attn = CausalSelfAttention(d, n_head)
        self.ln2 = nn.LayerNorm(d)
        self.mlp = nn.Sequential(
            nn.Linear(d, mlp_ratio * d), nn.GELU(),
            nn.Linear(mlp_ratio * d, d),
        )

    def forward(self, x):
        x = x + self.attn(self.ln1(x))
        x = x + self.mlp(self.ln2(x))
        return x

class GPT(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self, vocab, max_len, d=768, n_layer=12, n_head=12):
        super().__init__()
        self.tok = nn.Embedding(vocab, d)
        self.pos = nn.Embedding(max_len, d)
        self.blocks = nn.ModuleList(Block(d, n_head) for _ in range(n_layer))
        self.ln_f = nn.LayerNorm(d)
        self.head = nn.Linear(d, vocab, bias=False)
        self.head.weight = self.tok.weight   # weight tying

    def forward(self, idx, targets=None):
        B, L = idx.shape
        pos = torch.arange(L, device=idx.device)
        x = self.tok(idx) + self.pos(pos)
        for blk in self.blocks:
            x = blk(x)
        x = self.ln_f(x)
        logits = self.head(x)                         # (B, L, V)
        if targets is None:
            return logits, None
        loss = F.cross_entropy(logits.view(-1, logits.size(-1)),
                               targets.view(-1), ignore_index=-100)
        return logits, loss

    @torch.no_grad()
    def generate(self, idx, max_new_tokens, temperature=1.0, top_k=None):
        for _ in range(max_new_tokens):
            logits, _ = self(idx[:, -self.pos.num_embeddings:])
            logits = logits[:, -1, :] / temperature
            if top_k is not None:
                v, _ = torch.topk(logits, top_k)
                logits[logits < v[:, [-1]]] = -float("inf")
            probs = F.softmax(logits, dim=-1)
            next_id = torch.multinomial(probs, num_samples=1)
            idx = torch.cat((idx, next_id), dim=1)
        return idx

This is essentially Karpathy’s nanoGPT, modulo bias placements and biases. Replace LayerNorm with RMSNorm, GELU with SwiGLU, learned pos with RoPE inside attention, and you have a Llama-style architecture.

The residual stream. A useful conceptual frame (Elhage et al., 2021, “A Mathematical Framework for Transformer Circuits”). Each block reads from the residual stream, computes attention or MLP outputs, and writes back. The residual stream is a vector bus of dimension \(d\) that propagates through every layer. This view is essential for mechanistic interpretability (Section 12.2): “induction heads” can be described as a specific attention head reading a specific direction from the stream and writing another.

Model sizing rules of thumb (decoder-only).

Parameter count \(N\), given \(d_\text{model}\), \(n_\text{layer}\), \(V\), \(d_\text{ffn}\):

\[ N \approx V d + n_\text{layer}\big(4 d^2 + 2 d \cdot d_\text{ffn}\big) \]

The \(4 d^2\) accounts for the four projections (Q, K, V, O); \(2 d \cdot d_\text{ffn}\) for the two FFN projections. With SwiGLU it becomes \(3 d \cdot d_\text{ffn}\) and \(d_\text{ffn} \approx \frac{8}{3} d\) to compensate.

For Llama-3-8B: \(d=4096, n_\text{layer}=32, V=128256, d_\text{ffn}=14336\), \(\Rightarrow\) ~8B params (matches).

Pretraining compute budget — Chinchilla.

\(C \approx 6 N D\) where \(N\) is parameters and \(D\) is training tokens. Hoffmann et al.’s compute-optimal ratio is \(D \approx 20 N\) for compute-optimal training (e.g., a 7B model gets 140B tokens). Later models go far beyond this: Llama 3 8B was trained on 15T tokens — about 250× the compute-optimal ratio — because the inference savings of a small model are worth the extra training compute.

Common assessment traps.

  • Causal masking is essential. Without it the model trivially predicts the target.
  • Weight tying halves the embedding-parameter footprint at small vocabularies.
  • GPT is a transformer decoder without cross-attention. It is not “the decoder of T5”.
  • RoPE is applied inside attention, not added to embeddings.
  • The LM head’s output is logits, not probabilities; softmax happens inside the loss (or the decoding step), not the model.

PART 3 — Adapting and Tuning Models

A pretrained LLM is a knowledge-rich, instruction-naive base model. Adapting it to specific tasks, domains, styles, or human preferences is the entire purpose of post-training. Modern post-training is itself a multi-stage pipeline:

base model  →  continued pretraining (optional, domain-specific)
            →  supervised fine-tuning (SFT) on instruction data
            →  preference / RL stage (RLHF, DPO, IPO, KTO, RLAIF, Constitutional AI)
            →  rejection sampling / iterative refinement loops
            →  evaluation, safety tuning, red-teaming

This Part covers two Modules:

  • Module 5. Fine-tuning for tasks and domains, both full-parameter and parameter-efficient.
  • Module 6. Human and transfer learning — RLHF and its modern alternatives, plus the broader notion of transfer learning.

MODULE 5 — Fine-tuning for Tasks and Domains

5.1 Domain-Specific and Task-Specific Fine-tuning

Definition and motivation. Fine-tuning is taking a pretrained model and continuing training on a smaller, targeted dataset with the same (next-token) or a related objective. It bends the model toward a specific data distribution, style, or task. Three regimes are worth naming:

  1. Continued pretraining (CPT). Same objective as pretraining (next-token prediction) on a domain corpus — legal, biomedical, code, mathematics. Substantially improves performance in that domain at the cost of some general capability.
  2. Supervised fine-tuning (SFT). Train on (prompt, response) pairs, computing loss only on the response tokens. Used to teach the model to follow instructions, adopt chat conventions, or perform a specific task.
  3. Task-specific head fine-tuning. Attach a small classification or regression head to the model and train on labeled data for that task. Common with encoder-only models (BERT for classification); less common with modern decoder-only LLMs since prompt-based formulation is usually preferred.

Why fine-tune at all?

  • The base model’s capabilities are latent — it can do the task in principle but doesn’t reliably elicit them from prompts.
  • The base model speaks “completion,” not “answer.” Asking GPT-3 “What is the capital of France?” might be completed with “is a question that has been asked since 1789…” rather than “Paris.” Instruction tuning fixes this.
  • Domain vocabulary and style require examples in that domain; even a strong general model fails on highly specialized text.

Supervised fine-tuning — formulation.

Given a dataset \(\{(x_i, y_i)\}\) where \(x_i\) is the prompt and \(y_i\) is the desired response:

\[ \mathcal{L}_\text{SFT} = -\sum_i \sum_{t=1}^{|y_i|} \log p_\theta\bigl(y_{i,t} \mid x_i, y_{i,<t}\bigr) \]

Importantly, the loss is masked to the response tokens; the prompt is given but not predicted. In a chat template:

<|system|>You are a helpful assistant.<|end|>
<|user|>What is photosynthesis?<|end|>
<|assistant|>Photosynthesis is …<|end|>

Tokens up to and including the <|assistant|> tag have labels = -100 (ignored); the model is only graded on what comes after.

Choosing the right fine-tuning recipe.

Goal Recipe
Teach a new domain’s vocabulary and patterns Continued pretraining + SFT
Teach instruction-following Instruction-tuning SFT (Alpaca-style)
Teach a specific format (JSON, function call) SFT on examples of that format
Teach a chat persona / brand voice SFT on persona-conformant examples
Teach reasoning style (chain of thought) SFT on CoT-formatted responses; or distill from a stronger reasoner
Strong domain adaptation with new knowledge CPT first, then SFT
Lots of data, little compute PEFT (LoRA / QLoRA — Section 5.2)
Small model, plenty of GPU Full fine-tuning

Hyperparameters that matter.

  • Learning rate. Much smaller than pretraining: \(1\text{e-}5\) to \(5\text{e-}5\) for full fine-tuning of 7B-class models; \(1\text{e-}4\) to \(5\text{e-}4\) for LoRA. Larger LR for smaller models. A common heuristic: scale LR linearly with batch size; warmup over 3–10% of steps; cosine decay to ~10% of peak.
  • Batch size. Effective batch size 64–512 sequences (use gradient accumulation if GPU memory is tight). Too small → noisy gradients; too large → memorization.
  • Epochs. 1–3 for instruction tuning. More epochs overfit and degrade general capability. For very small SFT datasets (<1k examples) you may go to 5 epochs but verify on held-out.
  • Sequence length. Match the format. Pad or pack to keep utilization high.
  • Weight decay. 0.01–0.1; helps regularization.
  • Dropout. Usually 0; LLMs don’t dropout much, especially with the data scales now used.
  • Gradient clipping. Norm-clip at 1.0 (essential for stability).

Catastrophic forgetting. Fine-tuning shifts the model toward your data and can degrade unrelated capabilities. Mitigations:

  • Mix in general data. Add 5–30% generic instruction examples to your domain corpus.
  • Low learning rate and short training.
  • PEFT (Section 5.2) — modify a tiny subspace of weights so the rest is preserved.
  • Replay — periodically interleave samples from the original pretraining distribution.
  • Elastic Weight Consolidation (Kirkpatrick et al., 2017) — penalize weight changes proportional to the Fisher information of each parameter under the prior task.

Common failure modes.

  • Repetition / format degeneracy. Too few SFT examples and too long training collapse the model into a narrow output style.
  • Reward hacking before RL. SFT on rejection-sampled responses can amplify quirks of the scorer.
  • Privacy leakage. Memorization of small fine-tuning datasets — the model emits training examples verbatim. Aggressive dedup and limited epochs mitigate.
  • Mismatched template. If you fine-tune with one chat template and infer with another, performance silently collapses.

Worked example — full fine-tune a 7B model on instruction data.

import torch
from datasets import load_dataset
from transformers import (AutoModelForCausalLM, AutoTokenizer,
                          DataCollatorForLanguageModeling, Trainer, TrainingArguments)

model_id = "meta-llama/Meta-Llama-3-8B"
tok = AutoTokenizer.from_pretrained(model_id)
tok.pad_token = tok.eos_token
model = AutoModelForCausalLM.from_pretrained(model_id, torch_dtype=torch.bfloat16)

def format_example(ex):
    prompt = f"<|user|>{ex['instruction']}<|end|>\n<|assistant|>"
    full   = prompt + ex["output"] + "<|end|>"
    p_ids  = tok(prompt, add_special_tokens=False).input_ids
    f_ids  = tok(full,   add_special_tokens=False).input_ids
    labels = [-100]*len(p_ids) + f_ids[len(p_ids):]
    return {"input_ids": f_ids, "labels": labels}

ds = load_dataset("tatsu-lab/alpaca")["train"].map(format_example)

args = TrainingArguments(
    output_dir="ft-out",
    per_device_train_batch_size=2,
    gradient_accumulation_steps=16,    # eff. batch 32 per GPU
    learning_rate=2e-5,
    num_train_epochs=2,
    bf16=True,
    warmup_ratio=0.03,
    lr_scheduler_type="cosine",
    weight_decay=0.0,
    logging_steps=10,
    save_steps=500,
)

trainer = Trainer(model=model, args=args, train_dataset=ds, tokenizer=tok)
trainer.train()

Continued pretraining — a 1-paragraph sketch. Same code, but the dataset is raw unlabeled domain text packed into max-length sequences (e.g., 8k tokens each). Loss is computed across all tokens (no -100 mask). Use a smaller LR than original pretraining (1e-5 to 5e-5) and fewer epochs. A common recipe: 1–10 B tokens of domain data on a 7B model, mixed with a smaller fraction (5–20%) of generic data.

Common assessment traps.

  • Instruction tuning is SFT — these terms are often equated. Instruction tuning is a type of SFT where the dataset is (instruction, response) pairs.
  • CPT vs SFT. CPT changes the model’s knowledge; SFT changes the model’s behavior.
  • Fine-tuning is not RLHF. Fine-tuning is supervised; RLHF requires a reward model and a policy-gradient stage.

5.2 Adapter-Based and Parameter-Efficient Fine-tuning (PEFT)

Definition and motivation. Full fine-tuning a 7B-parameter model requires storing the model, the gradients (same size), and the optimizer state (2–8× the model size for Adam) — often >100 GB. For a 70B model, full fine-tuning needs a multi-GPU cluster.

Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT) restricts the trainable parameters to a tiny subset — usually 0.01–1% of the total — and freezes the rest. The motivation is threefold:

  1. Memory. Optimizer state only exists for trainable parameters.
  2. Storage. Each task’s “adapter” is a few MB instead of tens of GB.
  3. Modularity. Hundreds of adapters can be hot-swapped on a single base model.

PEFT methods fall into four families: reparameterization (LoRA, DoRA), additive adapters (Houlsby, Pfeiffer, IA3), prompt-based (prefix tuning, prompt tuning, P-tuning), and selective (BitFit).

(A) LoRA — Low-Rank Adaptation. Hu et al. (2021), “LoRA: Low-Rank Adaptation of Large Language Models.”

Idea. Freeze the pretrained weight matrix \(W \in \mathbb{R}^{d_\text{out}\times d_\text{in}}\) and add a low-rank update \(\Delta W = B A\) where \(A \in \mathbb{R}^{r \times d_\text{in}}\) and \(B \in \mathbb{R}^{d_\text{out}\times r}\) with \(r \ll \min(d_\text{in}, d_\text{out})\):

\[ W' = W + \alpha\cdot BA \]

During fine-tuning, only \(A\) and \(B\) are updated; \(W\) is frozen. At inference, \(BA\) can be merged into \(W\) — zero extra latency.

Why it works. Aghajanyan et al. (2020) and others showed that the intrinsic dimensionality of fine-tuning updates is low. A small rank suffices to express the task adaptation.

Parameter count. For a \(4096\times 4096\) matrix and \(r=8\): \(4096\cdot 8 + 8\cdot 4096 = 65{,}536\) params vs. \(16{,}777{,}216\) — a 256× reduction. Across a model with ~200 such matrices, LoRA typically trains 10–20M parameters versus 7B.

Hyperparameters.

  • Rank \(r\). Common values: 4, 8, 16, 32, 64. Larger \(r\) → more capacity, more memory.
  • Alpha \(\alpha\). Scaling factor; effective LR is \(\alpha/r\). Common: \(\alpha=2r\) or \(\alpha=16\).
  • Target modules. Which matrices to wrap. Common choices: q_proj and v_proj only (original LoRA), or all linear projections in attention and FFN. More targets → more capacity but more memory.
  • Dropout. LoRA-dropout in \(A\) before \(B\); 0.05–0.1.
  • Initialization. \(A\) is Kaiming-initialized; \(B\) is zero — so \(\Delta W = 0\) at the start of training.

Math view of training. The forward pass is

\[ y = Wx + (BA)x \cdot \frac{\alpha}{r} \]

Gradients flow only through \(A, B\). The pretrained \(W\) never receives a gradient.

Worked memory example. Fine-tuning Llama-3-8B with bf16 weights:

  • Model: 16 GB.
  • Full fine-tune optimizer state (Adam, fp32 master + 2 moments): ~96 GB.
  • LoRA with \(r=16\) on all linear modules: ~20 M trainable; optimizer state ~240 MB.

Total memory savings of ~95 GB for the optimizer alone.

Code — LoRA from scratch (PyTorch).

import torch.nn as nn

class LoRA(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self, base: nn.Linear, r=8, alpha=16, dropout=0.05):
        super().__init__()
        self.base = base
        d_in, d_out = base.in_features, base.out_features
        self.A = nn.Parameter(torch.empty(r, d_in));      nn.init.kaiming_uniform_(self.A, a=5**0.5)
        self.B = nn.Parameter(torch.zeros(d_out, r))
        self.scale = alpha / r
        self.dropout = nn.Dropout(dropout)
        for p in self.base.parameters():
            p.requires_grad = False

    def forward(self, x):
        return self.base(x) + (self.dropout(x) @ self.A.T @ self.B.T) * self.scale

(B) QLoRA — Quantized LoRA. Dettmers et al. (2023), “QLoRA: Efficient Finetuning of Quantized LLMs.”

Three innovations layered on LoRA:

  1. 4-bit NormalFloat (NF4) quantization for the base model. Information-theoretically optimal for normally-distributed weights.
  2. Double quantization — quantize the per-block quantization constants themselves (saving ~0.37 bits/param).
  3. Paged optimizer states — use CUDA unified memory to page Adam states between GPU and CPU when GPU OOMs.

QLoRA enables fine-tuning a 65B model on a single 48 GB GPU. The base model lives in NF4; only the LoRA adapters are trained in bf16. Quality is essentially indistinguishable from 16-bit LoRA fine-tuning, despite the 4-bit quantization.

(C) DoRA — Weight-Decomposed LoRA. Liu et al. (2024), “DoRA: Weight-Decomposed Low-Rank Adaptation.”

Decompose the weight into magnitude and direction:

\[ W = m \cdot \frac{V}{\|V\|} \]

LoRA updates only the direction \(V \to V + BA\); DoRA additionally trains the magnitude \(m\). Roughly doubles the effective rank without doubling parameters, and consistently outperforms LoRA in evaluations.

(D) IA3 — Infused Adapter by Inhibiting and Amplifying. Liu et al. (2022), “Few-Shot Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning is Better and Cheaper than In-Context Learning.”

Insert learned scaling vectors that multiply the keys, values, and FFN hidden activations:

\[ K' = l_k \odot K,\quad V' = l_v \odot V,\quad \text{FFN}_\text{hidden}' = l_\text{ff} \odot \text{FFN}_\text{hidden} \]

Trainable params: just three vectors per block — even less than LoRA.

(E) Adapters (Houlsby, Pfeiffer). Houlsby et al. (2019), “Parameter-Efficient Transfer Learning for NLP.”

Insert a small bottleneck MLP after each sublayer:

\[ h' = h + W_\text{up}\, \sigma(W_\text{down}\, h) \]

with \(W_\text{down}: d\to r\) and \(W_\text{up}: r\to d\). Two variants:

  • Houlsby: adapter after both attention and FFN.
  • Pfeiffer: adapter after FFN only.

Adds inference latency (the adapter MLP is non-mergeable). Largely superseded by LoRA, which can be merged at inference time.

(F) Prefix Tuning. Li & Liang (2021), “Prefix-Tuning: Optimizing Continuous Prompts for Generation.”

Prepend learned continuous vectors \(P_K, P_V \in \mathbb{R}^{L_p \times d}\) to the keys and values at every layer. The model attends to these “virtual tokens” as if they were part of the prompt, but they don’t go through the embedding table — they are learned directly in the K/V space.

(G) Prompt Tuning. Lester et al. (2021), “The Power of Scale for Parameter-Efficient Prompt Tuning.”

Learn a small embedding tensor \(P \in \mathbb{R}^{L_p \times d}\) and prepend it to the input embeddings. Only \(P\) is trained; the entire model is frozen. Works well at large scales (10B+), poorly at small ones.

(H) P-Tuning v2. Liu et al. (2021), “P-Tuning v2: Prompt Tuning Can Be Comparable to Fine-tuning Universally Across Scales and Tasks.”

Generalizes prompt tuning by adding learnable prompts at every layer (similar to prefix tuning), but reparameterizing them through a small MLP for stability.

(I) BitFit. Ben-Zaken et al. (2021), “BitFit: Simple Parameter-efficient Fine-tuning for Transformer-based Masked Language-models.”

Train only the bias terms of the network. Roughly 0.05% of parameters. Surprisingly competitive on classification; weaker on generation.

Choosing the right PEFT.

  • Default to LoRA / QLoRA — proven, mergeable, well-supported.
  • If you’re already on LoRA and want a small bump in quality: try DoRA.
  • If GPU memory is the bottleneck: QLoRA.
  • If you need thousands of swappable task adapters with minimal storage: IA3 or small-rank LoRA.
  • For research on continuous prompts: prefix tuning / P-Tuning v2.

Common assessment traps.

  • PEFT does not change inference quality of the base model on unrelated tasks — the base is frozen.
  • LoRA can be merged back into the base before deployment; adapters with non-linear components (Houlsby) cannot.
  • QLoRA quantizes the base model in 4-bit but the adapter is in bf16/fp16. Training is in 4-bit forward, bf16 LoRA update.
  • Prompt tuning != prompting. Prompt tuning learns continuous vectors that are not text; prompting writes text.

MODULE 6 — Human and Transfer Learning

6.1 Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) and Alternatives

Definition and motivation. RLHF is a three-stage pipeline that aligns a language model with human preferences. The base model is good at predicting tokens; RLHF is what makes it good at producing helpful, harmless, honest responses preferred by humans.

Christiano et al. (2017), “Deep Reinforcement Learning from Human Preferences,” introduced the technique for Atari and robotics. Ouyang et al. (2022, InstructGPT) applied it to LLMs at scale, demonstrating that a 1.3B InstructGPT model was preferred to a 175B base GPT-3 — the most cited single result showing why alignment matters more than scale alone.

The full pipeline.

1. Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT)
   Train base model on (prompt, demonstration) pairs to learn the chat / instruction format.

2. Reward Modeling (RM)
   Collect (prompt, response_A, response_B, label) triples where label says which response humans prefer.
   Train a reward model r_φ(prompt, response) → scalar.

3. RL fine-tuning
   Treat the SFT model as a policy π_θ.
   At each step, sample response y ~ π_θ(·|x).
   Receive reward r_φ(x,y).
   Update π_θ via PPO (or REINFORCE-with-baseline), with a KL penalty toward a reference policy π_ref.

The KL penalty \(\beta \cdot \mathrm{KL}(\pi_\theta\|\pi_\text{ref})\) keeps the policy near the SFT model, preventing it from “reward hacking” by exploiting RM blind spots.

Stage 1 — SFT. Already described in Section 5.1.

Stage 2 — Reward Modeling.

Data format. A prompt \(x\) and two responses \(y_w\) (winner) and \(y_l\) (loser), labeled by a human annotator who saw both.

Architecture. Take the SFT model, drop the LM head, replace with a scalar regression head \(r_\phi(x, y) \in \mathbb{R}\).

Loss — Bradley-Terry.

\[ \mathcal{L}_\text{RM} = -\mathbb{E}_{(x, y_w, y_l) \sim \mathcal{D}}\Bigl[\log \sigma\bigl(r_\phi(x, y_w) - r_\phi(x, y_l)\bigr)\Bigr] \]

This is a logistic loss on pairwise preferences. The model learns to assign higher scalar scores to preferred responses.

Properties.

  • Scale-invariant — only relative scores matter.
  • Trained with ~50k–500k preference pairs.
  • Larger reward models (1B–70B) often outperform smaller ones in alignment quality.
  • Calibration matters: the RM should produce smooth gradients in the regions the policy actually visits.

Stage 3 — PPO (Proximal Policy Optimization). Schulman et al. (2017), “Proximal Policy Optimization Algorithms.”

Objective. Maximize expected reward minus a KL penalty:

\[ \max_\theta\ \mathbb{E}_{x \sim \mathcal{D},\ y \sim \pi_\theta(\cdot|x)} \Bigl[ r_\phi(x, y) - \beta\, \log \frac{\pi_\theta(y|x)}{\pi_\text{ref}(y|x)} \Bigr] \]

In practice, the KL term is folded into a modified reward at each token:

\[ r_t = r_\phi(x, y) \cdot \mathbb{1}[t = T] - \beta\, \log\frac{\pi_\theta(y_t|x, y_{<t})}{\pi_\text{ref}(y_t|x, y_{<t})} \]

The terminal token receives the RM score; every token contributes a token-level KL penalty.

PPO clipped surrogate. Let \(\rho_t = \frac{\pi_\theta(y_t|s_t)}{\pi_{\theta_\text{old}}(y_t|s_t)}\) be the importance ratio. The PPO loss is:

\[ \mathcal{L}_\text{PPO} = -\mathbb{E}_t\Bigl[\min\bigl(\rho_t A_t,\ \text{clip}(\rho_t, 1-\epsilon, 1+\epsilon) A_t\bigr)\Bigr] \]

where \(A_t\) is the advantage (typically computed with GAE). The clip prevents the policy from moving too far in a single update — its namesake “proximal” property.

Practical considerations.

  • Four models in memory. PPO requires the policy, reference model (frozen SFT), reward model, and value model — all roughly the size of the base. This is why RLHF training is expensive.
  • KL coefficient \(\beta\). Typically 0.05–0.2. Too low: policy drifts and reward-hacks. Too high: no learning.
  • Generation hyperparameters during rollout. Temperature ~1.0; top-p 1.0. Diversity matters.
  • PPO epochs per batch. Usually 1–4.
  • Reward normalization. Subtract the running mean / divide by running std to keep rewards in a stable range.

Failure modes of RLHF.

  • Reward hacking. Policy finds patterns the RM scores high but humans dislike (overly long responses, excessive hedging, sycophancy).
  • Sycophancy. Model agrees with user-stated incorrect beliefs (Perez et al., 2022).
  • Distribution drift. Policy moves into regions the RM was not trained on; RM extrapolates badly.
  • Mode collapse. Policy converges to a narrow distribution of “safe” responses.
  • Training instability. Hyperparameter sensitivity is high.

Stage 3’ — DPO (Direct Preference Optimization). Rafailov et al. (2023), “Direct Preference Optimization: Your Language Model is Secretly a Reward Model.”

Insight. Under the Bradley-Terry preference model and a KL-regularized reward maximization objective, the optimal policy \(\pi^*\) satisfies:

\[ r(x, y) = \beta \log \frac{\pi^*(y|x)}{\pi_\text{ref}(y|x)} + \beta \log Z(x) \]

Plugging this into the Bradley-Terry preference loss yields a closed-form objective that does not need an explicit reward model, value model, or RL loop:

\[ \mathcal{L}_\text{DPO} = -\mathbb{E}_{(x, y_w, y_l)}\Bigl[\log \sigma\bigl(\beta\,\log\tfrac{\pi_\theta(y_w|x)}{\pi_\text{ref}(y_w|x)} - \beta\,\log\tfrac{\pi_\theta(y_l|x)}{\pi_\text{ref}(y_l|x)}\bigr)\Bigr] \]

Properties.

  • Two models in memory (policy and reference) — half of PPO.
  • Trains with the same preference dataset that an RM would use.
  • Drop-in replacement for PPO in most production pipelines (Llama 3, Tulu 3, Zephyr, OpenHermes — all use DPO).
  • \(\beta\) controls how far the policy can drift from reference (smaller \(\beta\) = more drift allowed).

Common \(\beta\) values. 0.01–0.5; tune on a held-out set.

Code skeleton.

def dpo_loss(policy_logp_w, policy_logp_l, ref_logp_w, ref_logp_l, beta=0.1):
    pi_logratios  = policy_logp_w - policy_logp_l
    ref_logratios = ref_logp_w    - ref_logp_l
    logits = beta * (pi_logratios - ref_logratios)
    return -F.logsigmoid(logits).mean()

policy_logp_w is the sum of log-probs that the policy assigns to the winner response tokens, similarly for the loser and the reference model.

Stage 3’’ — IPO (Identity Preference Optimization). Azar et al. (2023), “A General Theoretical Paradigm to Understand Learning from Human Preferences.”

Motivation. DPO can overfit to deterministic preference data (where the Bradley-Terry probability is 0/1). IPO replaces the logistic with a quadratic regression target:

\[ \mathcal{L}_\text{IPO} = \mathbb{E}_{(x,y_w,y_l)}\Bigl[\bigl(\log\tfrac{\pi_\theta(y_w|x)/\pi_\text{ref}(y_w|x)}{\pi_\theta(y_l|x)/\pi_\text{ref}(y_l|x)} - \tfrac{1}{2\beta}\bigr)^2\Bigr] \]

The target is a fixed margin; the optimization is more conservative.

Stage 3’’’ — KTO (Kahneman-Tversky Optimization). Ethayarajh et al. (2024), “KTO: Model Alignment as Prospect Theoretic Optimization.”

Motivation. Preference data is hard to collect; binary “thumbs up / down” is much easier. KTO uses unpaired desirable / undesirable examples and a Kahneman-Tversky-style asymmetric utility:

\[ \mathcal{L}_\text{KTO} = \mathbb{E}[\lambda_d (1 - \sigma(\beta z_d))] + \mathbb{E}[\lambda_u (1 - \sigma(-\beta z_u))] \]

with reference-relative log ratios \(z\). Practical when you have many ratings but few paired comparisons.

RLAIF — RL from AI Feedback. Bai et al. (2022), “Constitutional AI”; Lee et al. (2023), “RLAIF: Scaling Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback with AI Feedback.”

Replace the human annotator in the preference-collection step with an LLM judge prompted with a rubric. The LLM compares two responses and selects a winner. Properties:

  • Scales: a million preference labels in a day with minimal cost.
  • Calibrated: depends on the judge LLM’s quality.
  • Bias-prone: inherits the judge’s preferences (verbosity, format).
  • Standard practice at major labs as a high-coverage complement to (smaller) human labels.

Constitutional AI. Bai et al. (2022), Anthropic.

  1. Write a “constitution” — a list of principles the model should follow (helpful, harmless, honest, etc.).
  2. Self-critique stage. Generate a response; ask the model to critique it against the constitution; ask the model to revise.
  3. Train the policy on the revised responses (SFT).
  4. Preference stage. Generate two responses; ask the model to pick the better one per the constitution; train an RM (or use DPO directly) on these AI-generated preferences.

Produces “Claude-style” models without relying on humans for every preference.

Common assessment traps.

  • RLHF has three stages, not one (SFT + RM + RL).
  • DPO removes the RM and the RL loop — it is a direct preference loss.
  • The reference model in DPO/PPO is the SFT model, not the base.
  • The KL penalty in RLHF prevents reward hacking, not memorization.
  • Constitutional AI is RLAIF (or its predecessor) — same family.

6.2 Transfer Learning with Pretrained LLMs

Definition and motivation. Transfer learning is the general principle of reusing a model trained on a source task / domain to bootstrap a model for a target task / domain. In modern LLMs, transfer learning is the default — pretraining is the (very expensive) source task; everything afterwards (CPT, SFT, PEFT, RL, prompting) is a form of transfer.

The pretrain → fine-tune paradigm is itself a form of transfer learning. The broader picture includes:

Transfer modality Example
Pretrain → fine-tune Llama-3-base → Llama-3-Instruct
Pretrain → prompt (no weight updates) Few-shot prompting GPT-4
Pretrain → CPT → SFT Code Llama: Llama 2 → CPT on code → SFT on code instructions
Pretrain → distill LLaMA → Alpaca (Stanford); GPT-4 → student model
Pretrain → PEFT Llama-3-base + LoRA adapter for legal Q&A
Cross-modality transfer BERT text encoder reused inside CLIP, BLIP-2, LLaVA
Cross-lingual transfer mBERT → fine-tune on Hindi sentiment

Why transfer learning works for LLMs.

  1. Pretraining instills enormous breadth of knowledge that’s hard to recover from any task-specific dataset alone.
  2. The pretraining objective (next-token prediction) is general enough that many downstream tasks can be cast in its mold.
  3. Representations learned at scale generalize — internal features (named entities, syntactic categories, world knowledge) transfer to many tasks (Tenney et al., 2019; Rogers et al., 2020).

Five transfer strategies, ranked by cost.

  1. Zero-shot prompting. Just ask the model. No weight updates. Surprisingly competitive at scale.
  2. Few-shot / in-context learning (ICL). Put \(k\) examples in the prompt before the query. Brown et al. (2020) showed this works for GPT-3.
  3. Instruction tuning. SFT on instruction data — once — and then use the resulting model for many downstream tasks zero-shot. The standard modern recipe.
  4. PEFT. When a particular downstream task needs more than instructions can give, train LoRA / QLoRA adapters.
  5. Full fine-tuning. Reserved for cases where PEFT is insufficient and the budget exists.

Negative transfer. Fine-tuning on one task can hurt performance on others (catastrophic forgetting). Mitigations:

  • Data mixing. Combine task-specific data with generic data.
  • PEFT. Modular adapters per task limit interference.
  • Multi-task learning. Train on a balanced mixture of tasks simultaneously (T5’s recipe).
  • Distillation. A teacher general LLM distills into a student that retains both capabilities.

Cross-lingual transfer. mBERT, XLM-R, NLLB, and other multilingual encoders learn aligned representations across 100+ languages. A downstream classifier trained on English data can perform on Hindi, Swahili, or Vietnamese — often within a few points of in-language training, given a large multilingual base.

Cross-modal transfer. CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) demonstrated that a text encoder, when contrastively aligned with an image encoder, learns image representations that transfer to many vision tasks. Similarly, vision-language models reuse strong text LLMs as the language backbone.

Common assessment traps.

  • Transfer learning ≠ fine-tuning. Fine-tuning is one form of transfer. Prompting is another.
  • In-context learning is transfer learning without weight updates.
  • Distillation transfers capabilities from teacher to student through generated data, not direct weight copying.
  • PEFT preserves the source-task capabilities (the base is frozen) — this is why it transfers safely.

MODULE 7 — Reducing Model Complexity

7.1 Model Quantization

Definition and motivation. Quantization is the process of representing model weights (and optionally activations and the KV cache) with fewer bits than fp32. The motivation is concrete and unforgiving:

  • A 70B-parameter model in fp16 is 140 GB. In int4 it is 35 GB — fits on a single H100.
  • Memory bandwidth, not compute, is the bottleneck in LLM inference. Halving bits halves bandwidth and roughly doubles throughput.
  • Lower precision arithmetic also reduces FLOPs in some cases (FP8 on H100).

The risk: accuracy degradation. Most modern quantization work focuses on getting from int4 down without measurable quality loss.

Numeric formats.

Format Bits Range Notes
FP32 32 \(\pm 3.4\text{e}{38}\) Training reference
FP16 (IEEE half) 16 \(\pm 65{,}504\) Limited range, overflows easily
BF16 (Brain Float) 16 Same exponent range as FP32 Trades mantissa for range; default for modern LLMs
FP8 (E4M3, E5M2) 8 E5M2 has FP16-like range; E4M3 has more precision H100 Tensor Cores; nascent at training
INT8 8 -128 to 127 Common inference quantization
INT4 4 -8 to 7 (signed) or 0 to 15 (unsigned) Standard for deploy
NF4 4 Non-uniform quantiles of the standard normal QLoRA; information-optimal for normal weights
INT3, INT2 3, 2 very small Lossy; for extreme memory

Quantization granularity.

  • Per-tensor. One scale (and zero point) per tensor.
  • Per-channel (per output channel). One scale per row; better accuracy on linear layers.
  • Per-group / blockwise. Scales per group of \(k\) values (e.g., \(k=64, 128\)). The QLoRA / GPTQ / AWQ default.

Quantization formula (linear, asymmetric).

\[ \hat{x} = \operatorname{round}\!\left(\frac{x - z}{s}\right),\quad x \approx s\cdot \hat{x} + z \]

\(s\) is the scale, \(z\) the zero point. For symmetric quantization, \(z=0\).

PTQ vs QAT.

  • Post-Training Quantization (PTQ). Take a trained fp16 model, compute scales on a small calibration set, quantize. Cheap (minutes to hours). Quality may drop, especially at 4 bits or below.
  • Quantization-Aware Training (QAT). Insert “fake quantize” ops in the forward pass during training, so gradients see the rounding. Recovers more accuracy at low bits but requires a training run. Used for very low-bit deployments (int4/int2 with critical accuracy needs).

For LLMs, almost all production quantization is PTQ — training-from-scratch in low precision is rare; QAT is occasionally used as a finetune.

(A) bitsandbytes — LLM.int8() and 4-bit (NF4, FP4). Dettmers et al. (2022, “LLM.int8()”); Dettmers et al. (2023, QLoRA).

LLM.int8() quantizes weights to int8 and uses mixed decomposition: outlier features (a small fraction with magnitudes > 6) are kept in fp16; the rest in int8. This preserves accuracy without specialized calibration. Drop-in via transformers:

from transformers import AutoModelForCausalLM, BitsAndBytesConfig
m = AutoModelForCausalLM.from_pretrained(
    "meta-llama/Meta-Llama-3-8B", quantization_config=BitsAndBytesConfig(load_in_8bit=True)
)

NF4 is the QLoRA default — 4-bit non-uniform quantiles of \(\mathcal{N}(0,1)\), optimal for normal-distributed weights. Per-block scales (block size 64) + double quantization (quantize the scales again with 8-bit). Achieves near-fp16 accuracy at 4× memory reduction.

(B) GPTQ. Frantar et al. (2022), “GPTQ: Accurate Post-Training Quantization for Generative Pre-trained Transformers.”

Idea. Quantize each weight matrix column-by-column, using a small calibration set, while updating the remaining unquantized weights to compensate for the introduced error. Based on Optimal Brain Surgeon (OBS) / Optimal Brain Compression.

Algorithm sketch.

  1. Compute the empirical Hessian \(H = X X^\top\) of the input activations on the calibration set.
  2. For each output channel, iterate over input columns left-to-right:
    • Quantize column \(j\).
    • Update all columns \(>j\) by \(-\frac{\Delta w_j}{H_{jj}} H_{j,k}\) to absorb the quantization error.

Yields 4-bit weights with minimal perplexity increase. Compatible with int3/int2 with mild loss. Standard for vLLM and text-generation-inference deployments.

(C) AWQ — Activation-aware Weight Quantization. Lin et al. (2023), “AWQ: Activation-aware Weight Quantization for LLM Compression and Acceleration.”

Insight. Not all weights are equal. The most important ones are those that multiply large-magnitude activations. Protect those: scale the corresponding input channels up before quantization, scale the weights down by the same factor — preserving the product while reducing quantization error on the critical weights.

\[ y = (\text{scale} \cdot x) \cdot (W / \text{scale}) \]

The scale is chosen per-channel to minimize quantization error of the salient weights. No backpropagation needed. Comparable or better than GPTQ at int4, faster to compute, robust on instruction-tuned models.

(D) SmoothQuant. Xiao et al. (2023), “SmoothQuant: Accurate and Efficient Post-Training Quantization for Large Language Models.”

Insight. LLM activations have a few channels with very high dynamic range, making activation quantization (necessary for int8 GEMM) hard. SmoothQuant “smooths” these outliers by migrating dynamic range from activations to weights:

\[ y = (\text{diag}(s)^{-1} X)\cdot(\text{diag}(s) W) \]

with per-channel \(s = \max(|X|)^\alpha / \max(|W|)^{1-\alpha}\). After the rebalance, both \(X'\) and \(W'\) are quantization-friendly.

Pairs well with W8A8 (both weights and activations in int8) for ~2× speedup. Frequently used with TensorRT-LLM.

(E) FP8 (H100, B100). Micikevicius et al. (2022), “FP8 Formats for Deep Learning.”

Two formats:

  • E4M3 (4 exponent, 3 mantissa) — more precision, less range.
  • E5M2 (5 exponent, 2 mantissa) — same range as fp16, less precision.

H100 Tensor Cores support FP8 GEMM natively at ~2× FP16 throughput. Use E4M3 for forward pass and weights, E5M2 for backward (similar to FP16/BF16 split).

(F) GGUF / llama.cpp quantization. ggerganov’s GGUF format ships dozens of named quantization schemes (Q2_K, Q3_K_M, Q4_K_M, Q5_K_M, Q6_K, Q8_0, …). The _K variants use K-quants — blockwise, super-block scales — designed for CPU inference. Q4_K_M is the canonical “good enough” Llama deploy on consumer hardware.

Worked memory example.

Llama-3-70B parameters: 70B.

  • FP32: 280 GB.
  • FP16/BF16: 140 GB.
  • INT8: 70 GB.
  • NF4 / INT4: 35 GB.
  • INT3: ~26 GB.

KV cache for an 8k-token context in fp16: ~10 GB (Llama-3-70B with GQA). KV cache quantized to int8: ~5 GB. KV cache to int4: ~2.5 GB.

Common assessment traps.

  • PTQ vs QAT. PTQ quantizes after training; QAT trains with simulated quantization in the forward pass.
  • GPTQ, AWQ, and SmoothQuant are PTQ methods.
  • NF4 is for weights, not activations.
  • W8A8 (SmoothQuant) = weights and activations both int8. W4A16 (GPTQ/AWQ default) = weights int4, activations fp16.
  • Quantization saves memory and bandwidth; it may or may not save FLOPs depending on hardware (int4 GEMM is only fast on supported kernels).

7.2 Knowledge Distillation

Definition and motivation. Knowledge distillation (KD) — Hinton, Vinyals, Dean (2015), “Distilling the Knowledge in a Neural Network” — trains a student model to mimic a teacher model. The student is typically smaller (fewer parameters, fewer layers, fewer heads). The goal is to retain most of the teacher’s accuracy at a fraction of the cost.

Why KD works: the teacher’s soft outputs contain more information than hard labels. The distribution over wrong classes carries a “dark knowledge” signal — which wrong answers are more similar than others.

Classical KD loss.

\[ \mathcal{L}_\text{KD} = \alpha\, T^2 \cdot \mathrm{KL}\!\left(p_\text{teacher}^{(T)} \,\|\, p_\text{student}^{(T)}\right) + (1-\alpha)\, \mathcal{L}_\text{CE}(p_\text{student}, y_\text{true}) \]

where \(p^{(T)}\) denotes softmax with temperature \(T\) (higher \(T\) = softer):

\[ p_i^{(T)} = \frac{\exp(z_i/T)}{\sum_j \exp(z_j/T)} \]

The \(T^2\) factor compensates for the gradient shrinkage caused by the temperature. \(\alpha\) typically 0.5–0.9.

Types of KD for LLMs.

  1. Logit-level KD (classical). Student matches teacher’s full token-distribution.
  2. Feature-level KD. Student’s hidden states / attention maps mimic the teacher’s at corresponding layers. Requires aligned architectures.
  3. Attention-distillation (MiniLM). Student matches teacher’s attention scores or value relations rather than logits.
  4. Sequence-level KD (Kim & Rush 2016). Student trained on teacher-generated sequences instead of (or in addition to) teacher distributions per step. Cheap and effective.
  5. On-policy KD. Student samples its own output; teacher scores; student updated to match. Helps with exposure bias.
  6. MiniLLM (reverse KL). Gu et al. (2024) showed that for generative LLMs, reverse-KL (\(\mathrm{KL}(\pi_\text{student} \| \pi_\text{teacher})\)) outperforms forward-KL by avoiding mode-covering behavior.

Canonical distilled models.

  • DistilBERT (Sanh et al., 2019) — half-size BERT (6 layers vs 12) trained on (a) teacher’s MLM logits + (b) classical MLM + (c) hidden-state cosine similarity. Achieves ~97% of BERT’s GLUE score at 60% the parameters and 60% the inference time.
  • TinyBERT (Jiao et al., 2019) — two-stage distillation (general + task-specific) with attention and hidden-state alignment.
  • MiniLM (Wang et al., 2020) — uses self-attention distribution and value-relation distillation rather than hidden states; works for arbitrary student architectures.
  • MobileBERT (Sun et al., 2020) — much smaller student via bottleneck layers; trained with feature transfer + attention transfer.
  • TinyLlama (Zhang et al., 2024) — 1.1B Llama-style model trained from scratch on 3 T tokens; not a strict distill but heavily inspired.
  • Distilled GPT-3.5 → Llama (Alpaca, Vicuna) — Stanford Alpaca / Vicuna used teacher-generated SFT examples to distill GPT-3.5’s instruction-following into Llama-7B/13B.

Worked example — DistilBERT loss.

def distill_loss(student_logits, teacher_logits, labels, T=2.0, alpha=0.5):
    soft_t = F.softmax(teacher_logits / T, dim=-1)
    log_soft_s = F.log_softmax(student_logits / T, dim=-1)
    kd = F.kl_div(log_soft_s, soft_t, reduction="batchmean") * (T ** 2)
    ce = F.cross_entropy(student_logits, labels)
    return alpha * kd + (1 - alpha) * ce

Sequence-level KD (concise recipe).

  1. Run the teacher to generate \(N\) sequences per prompt (often \(N=1\)).
  2. Build a dataset of (prompt, teacher_generated_sequence).
  3. Train the student with the standard SFT loss on this dataset.

This is exactly how Alpaca was built. The cost is the teacher inference; the student training is ordinary SFT.

When to distill, when to quantize.

Goal Recipe
Same model, smaller memory and faster inference Quantize
Smaller model architecture, retain most quality Distill
Best of both Distill, then quantize (or QLoRA on a distilled student)

Common assessment traps.

  • Distillation transfers behavior, not weights — the student architecture can differ from the teacher.
  • Temperature in KD softens the teacher distribution, exposing the dark knowledge.
  • DistilBERT loss combines KD, MLM, and cosine alignment — not just KL.
  • Synthetic data generation (Module 2.2) is a form of distillation when the generator is a stronger LLM.

MODULE 8 — Accelerating Training and Inference

8.1 Mixed-Precision Training

Definition and motivation. Mixed-precision training (Micikevicius et al., 2018) uses 16-bit floats (FP16 or BF16) for most arithmetic while keeping a 32-bit copy of the master weights for the optimizer. The goal is to halve memory and roughly double compute throughput on hardware with 16-bit Tensor Cores (V100, A100, H100, B100).

Why pure FP16 fails for training.

  • FP16 has only 5-bit exponent, range \(\pm 65{,}504\) — gradients underflow.
  • Without a 32-bit master weight, weight updates of magnitude \(< 6\text{e}{-8}\) are rounded to zero — training stalls.

Fix — three components.

  1. Master weights in FP32. Forward and backward in FP16/BF16; the optimizer keeps FP32 weights and applies the FP32 weight update.

  2. Loss scaling. Multiply the loss by \(s \approx 2^{10}-2^{15}\) before backprop; divide gradients by \(s\) before the optimizer step. This shifts gradient magnitudes into the FP16 representable range, avoiding underflow. Dynamic loss scaling (PyTorch AMP, Apex) starts at a high \(s\), halves on any NaN/Inf in the gradients, doubles after \(N\) good steps.

  3. FP32 accumulation. GEMMs are FP16 inputs but the accumulator is FP32 — preserving precision in summed dot products. This is built into Tensor Cores.

BF16 vs FP16.

  • BF16 has 8-bit exponent (same as FP32) but only 7-bit mantissa. Range is FP32-like; precision is worse than FP16.
  • Because BF16’s range matches FP32, no loss scaling is needed. Modern LLM training (Llama, Mistral, PaLM) is almost universally BF16 on A100/H100.
  • FP16 with loss scaling is still used on hardware where BF16 isn’t supported.

FP8 training. H100/B100 supports FP8 (E4M3 + E5M2). Stable training requires per-tensor scaling, mixed E4M3/E5M2 (forward vs backward), and careful gradient handling. NVIDIA Transformer Engine provides this. FP8 GEMMs are ~2× faster than BF16 on H100. Used in production by some frontier teams.

Code — automatic mixed precision in PyTorch.

scaler = torch.cuda.amp.GradScaler()        # for fp16 only; bf16 needs no scaler

for x, y in loader:
    optimizer.zero_grad()
    with torch.cuda.amp.autocast(dtype=torch.bfloat16):
        out = model(x)
        loss = criterion(out, y)
    if scaler.is_enabled():
        scaler.scale(loss).backward()
        scaler.step(optimizer)
        scaler.update()
    else:
        loss.backward()
        optimizer.step()

Memory savings (training). Suppose a 7B model trained with vanilla AdamW in FP32:

  • Weights: 28 GB.
  • Gradients: 28 GB.
  • Optimizer state (2 moments): 56 GB. Total ~112 GB.

Mixed precision (BF16 weights, BF16 grads, FP32 optimizer state):

  • BF16 weights: 14 GB.
  • BF16 grads: 14 GB.
  • FP32 weights master + optimizer state (3 × 28 GB): 84 GB.
  • Total ~112 GB. (Note: master copy + optimizer state actually dominate; total is similar.)

The actual savings from mixed precision are mostly in compute throughput and activation memory, not in the parameter+optimizer footprint. To reduce optimizer-state memory, use ZeRO / FSDP / 8-bit optimizers (Section 8.2).

8-bit optimizers. bitsandbytes Adam-8bit (Dettmers et al., 2021) stores moments in 8-bit with blockwise quantization. Reduces optimizer state from 56 GB to ~14 GB for a 7B model — usually loss-of-quality-free for LLMs.

Common assessment traps.

  • BF16 needs no loss scaling. FP16 does.
  • Master weights are FP32, not the activations.
  • Mixed precision is not the same as quantization. Quantization is integer; mixed precision is float-of-different-widths.

8.2 Parallelism and GPU Usage

LLM training and inference at scale require splitting the computation across many GPUs. Six distinct parallelism axes are in modern use, and frontier systems combine several.

Axis What’s split Comms cost Used for
Data parallelism (DP) Batch dim, weights replicated All-reduce on gradients Default at any scale
ZeRO 1/2/3 Optimizer / gradients / weights across DP ranks All-gather + reduce-scatter Memory savings at DP
FSDP Weights sharded across DP ranks Same as ZeRO-3 PyTorch’s ZeRO-3
Tensor parallel (TP) Each matrix split across GPUs All-reduce within layer Within-server (NVLink)
Pipeline parallel (PP) Layers split across GPUs Activations between stages Cross-server
Sequence/Context parallel Sequence dimension Ring all-gather Long contexts
Expert parallel (EP) MoE experts across GPUs All-to-all token shuffles MoE models

A modern 1000-GPU training run might combine TP=8 (within node) × PP=4 × DP=32 × ZeRO-1, plus sequence parallelism for activations.

Data parallelism (DP). Each GPU holds a full replica of the model and processes a different shard of the batch. Gradients are averaged via all_reduce after the backward pass. Simple but memory-inefficient (model fully replicated).

[GPU 0] full model | batch shard 0
[GPU 1] full model | batch shard 1
[GPU N] full model | batch shard N
            ↓ all-reduce gradients
all GPUs apply same update

ZeRO (DeepSpeed). Rajbhandari et al. (2020), “ZeRO: Memory Optimizations Toward Training A Trillion Parameter Models.”

Three stages of progressively aggressive sharding across data-parallel ranks:

  • ZeRO-1: shard optimizer states (Adam moments). Largest practical win for free.
  • ZeRO-2: shard optimizer states + gradients.
  • ZeRO-3: shard everything — optimizer, gradients, and weights. Each GPU only holds a slice; weights are gathered on demand for forward / backward and freed after use.

ZeRO-3 enables training models larger than a single GPU’s memory by splitting them across DP ranks.

Memory savings (formal): Adam’s optimizer state is \(12N\) bytes (2 fp32 moments + master weights for \(N\) fp16 params, less for 8-bit). ZeRO-1 reduces this from \(12N\) to \(12N/d\) (with \(d\) DP ranks). ZeRO-3 reduces all weight + grad + opt state to \(\sim 16N/d\).

Communication. ZeRO-3 increases comm volume (an extra all-gather of weights at each forward/backward), but with NVLink / InfiniBand this is usually cheaper than spilling activations to CPU.

FSDP (PyTorch FullyShardedDataParallel). PyTorch’s native equivalent of ZeRO-3. Wraps each transformer block as a “shard unit”; the wrapping is recursive. Forward unwraps the parameters (all_gather), computes, then re-shards. Backward unshards, computes the local gradients, reduces (reduce_scatter), and reshards.

Example:

from torch.distributed.fsdp import FullyShardedDataParallel as FSDP
from torch.distributed.fsdp.wrap import transformer_auto_wrap_policy

model = FSDP(model,
    auto_wrap_policy=functools.partial(transformer_auto_wrap_policy,
                                       transformer_layer_cls={GPTBlock}),
    mixed_precision=MixedPrecision(param_dtype=torch.bfloat16,
                                   reduce_dtype=torch.bfloat16,
                                   buffer_dtype=torch.bfloat16),
    sharding_strategy=ShardingStrategy.FULL_SHARD,    # ZeRO-3 equivalent
)

Tensor parallelism (TP). Megatron-LM (Shoeybi et al., 2019). Split each weight matrix across GPUs.

For an FFN \(y = X W_1 W_2\):

  • Column-parallel \(W_1\): split along output dim. Each GPU computes a slice of activations.
  • Row-parallel \(W_2\): split along input dim. Each GPU computes a partial product; all_reduce to sum.

For attention: split heads across GPUs (each GPU does some heads); the output projection is row-parallel and requires an all_reduce.

Cost. Two all-reduces per transformer block. Comms volume is large; TP is only practical within a node (NVLink, 600+ GB/s).

Sweet spot. TP=8 inside an 8-GPU H100 node, scaling to larger systems via PP and DP.

Pipeline parallelism (PP). Split layers across GPUs: GPU 0 holds layers 0-7, GPU 1 holds 8-15, etc. The forward pass flows through the stages; the backward pass flows back.

Naïve pipelining wastes time (a stage idles when waiting). GPipe and 1F1B (PipeDream) interleave micro-batches so each stage is always busy. Megatron’s interleaved schedule further subdivides layers among GPUs for better balance.

Bubble. Idle time at start (forward fill) and end (backward drain). With \(m\) micro-batches and \(p\) stages, the bubble fraction is \((p-1)/(m+p-1)\). Want many micro-batches per global batch.

Sequence / context parallelism. Split the sequence dimension across GPUs. Each GPU sees a chunk of tokens. For attention, where every token interacts with every other token, ring-based communication exchanges keys/values around the ring (Liu et al., 2023, “Ring Attention”). Enables million-token contexts. Used in Gemini Long Context, Llama 3 Long Context, and recent open work.

Expert parallelism (EP). For MoE models, place different experts on different GPUs. Routing dispatches each token to its assigned expert(s), which requires an all_to_all token shuffle: tokens to their expert, then results back. Very high comm volume but tolerated because MoE compute per token is also lower.

Gradient checkpointing (activation checkpointing). Chen et al. (2016).

Problem. Activations dominate memory in long-context / large-batch training. Storing every layer’s activation for the backward pass costs \(O(n_\text{layers}\cdot L \cdot d)\).

Solution. Don’t store all activations. At forward, store only at “checkpoint” boundaries (e.g., per transformer block). At backward, recompute the activations between the previous checkpoint and the current step on the fly.

Cost: extra forward compute (typically ~33% more total compute). Benefit: linear-in-layers activation memory drops to \(O(\sqrt{n_\text{layers}})\) or even \(O(1)\).

from torch.utils.checkpoint import checkpoint
def block_forward(x, block):
    return block(x)
for block in blocks:
    x = checkpoint(block_forward, x, block, use_reentrant=False)

FlashAttention (Module 3.2 recap). Recompute attention without ever materializing the \(L \times L\) matrix; tile \(Q, K, V\) into SRAM, run a streaming softmax. 2–10× faster, \(O(L)\) memory. The single biggest free speedup for long-context training and inference. Standard in modern stacks; PyTorch ships it as scaled_dot_product_attention.

Kernel fusion. Combine consecutive ops into a single CUDA kernel to avoid HBM round-trips. Examples: fused RMSNorm + matmul, fused softmax, fused SwiGLU, fused RoPE + attention. Triton makes writing custom fused kernels accessible.

KV cache. Already covered in Module 3 — cache \(K, V\) tensors at inference so each new token costs \(O(L d)\) rather than \(O(L^2 d)\).

Paged attention (vLLM). Kwon et al. (2023), “Efficient Memory Management for Large Language Model Serving with PagedAttention.”

Problem. Naïve KV cache reserves contiguous memory for the max prompt length per request. This wastes memory (under-utilized blocks) and forces request-level reservation.

Solution. Treat KV cache like virtual memory: split into fixed-size blocks (e.g., 16 tokens). Each request’s logical KV sequence maps to physical blocks via a block table — like OS paging. Three benefits:

  1. No internal fragmentation. Memory wasted per request reduces to <1 block.
  2. Sharing. Prefixes (system prompts) can be shared across requests by mapping their blocks.
  3. Throughput. Higher batch sizes possible because the same memory hosts more requests.

vLLM (the serving system that introduced this) is the de facto standard for high-throughput LLM serving today.

Speculative decoding. Leviathan, Kalman & Matias (2022) “Fast Inference from Transformers via Speculative Decoding”; Chen et al. (2023) “Accelerating Large Language Model Decoding with Speculative Sampling.”

Idea. Run a small, cheap “draft” model to propose \(k\) candidate tokens at once. Then run the large “verifier” model on the proposed sequence in a single forward pass (cheap because it’s the same prompt + \(k\) tokens). For each draft token, accept or reject based on the ratio of verifier vs. draft probabilities. On rejection, sample the next token from a corrected distribution. Statistically equivalent to sampling from the large model.

  • Speedup. 2–4× wall-clock for typical models when the draft has a ~70% accept rate.
  • No quality loss — the output distribution is exactly the large model’s.
  • Draft model. Either a small dedicated model (TinyLlama for Llama, or a Llama-3-8B drafting for Llama-3-70B), or a model “self-drafted” via Medusa heads, Lookahead Decoding, or n-gram skipping (Eagle, ReDrafter).

Continuous batching (vLLM, TGI). Traditional batching waits for the slowest request in a batch to finish before processing the next batch. Continuous batching processes each token step in parallel across requests, removing finished requests and adding new ones every step. Combined with paged attention, this dramatically improves GPU utilization.

  • Tensor parallel splits weights inside a layer; pipeline parallel splits layers across stages.
  • Gradient checkpointing trades compute for memory (~33% more compute, much less activation memory).
  • Speculative decoding is exact — it does not approximate the large model’s output distribution.
  • Paged attention is about KV-cache memory layout, not about attention math.

PART 5 — Controlling and Generating Outputs

A trained LLM produces a probability distribution over the vocabulary at each step. Decoding is the rule that turns those distributions into a specific output sequence. Prompting is the design of inputs that shape what the LLM produces. Both are inference-time techniques — no weight updates required — and together they determine 80% of what end users see.

This Part covers two Modules:

  • Module 9. Decoding strategies — beam search, top-k, top-p, temperature, typical sampling, min-p, repetition penalty, contrastive decoding.
  • Module 10. Prompt-based behaviors — zero-shot, few-shot, chain-of-thought, self-consistency, Tree-of-Thoughts, ReAct, Reflexion, program-of-thought, prompt chaining, function calling, tool use.

MODULE 9 — Applying Decoding Strategies

9.1 Beam Search for Sequence Generation

Definition and motivation. Beam search is a deterministic, breadth-limited search over the space of output sequences. At each step it keeps the \(B\) highest-scoring partial sequences (the beams) and expands each by appending every possible next token, scoring the resulting \(B \cdot |V|\) candidates and keeping the top-\(B\) for the next step. Continues until all beams hit an end-of-sequence token (or a maximum length).

Beam search is the natural choice for tasks with a clearly correct answer: translation, summarization, code completion. It is rarely used for open-ended generation (chat, creative writing) because it produces overly safe, repetitive text and lacks diversity.

Greedy decoding is the special case \(B=1\) — always pick the single highest-probability next token. Fast but quality is brittle.

Algorithm.

beams = [(empty_sequence, score=0)]
while not all beams ended:
    new_beams = []
    for (seq, score) in beams:
        if seq ends with <eos>: keep seq; continue
        logits = model(seq)[-1]                 # next-token logits
        log_probs = log_softmax(logits)
        for token, lp in top_B(log_probs):
            new_beams.append((seq + [token], score + lp))
    beams = top_B(new_beams)
return beams[0]

Scoring. The natural score is the joint log-likelihood:

\[ \log p(y_1, \dots, y_T \mid x) = \sum_{t=1}^T \log p(y_t \mid x, y_{<t}) \]

Length bias. Joint log-likelihood is monotonically decreasing in length — every appended token has \(\log p < 0\). Beam search systematically prefers shorter sequences. Length normalization divides by length raised to a hyperparameter \(\alpha\) (Wu et al., 2016, GNMT):

\[ \text{score} = \frac{\log p(y \mid x)}{|y|^\alpha},\quad \alpha \in [0.6, 1.0] \]

Repetition. Beam search produces repetitive output (“text degeneration”, Holtzman et al., 2019). Common mitigations:

  • N-gram blocking. Forbid any beam from generating an \(n\)-gram (typically \(n=3\)) it has already emitted.
  • Repetition penalty. Divide the logit of any already-emitted token by \(r > 1\) (commonly 1.1–1.3).
  • Diverse Beam Search (Vijayakumar et al., 2018). Run \(G\) groups of beams in parallel; each group is penalized for resembling the others.

Worked example. Imagine a tiny model and beam width \(B=2\):

  • Step 1: logits → top-2 tokens “I” (lp = -0.2) and “The” (lp = -0.3). Beams: [("I", -0.2), ("The", -0.3)].
  • Step 2: from “I”: top-2 are “love” (-0.1), “see” (-0.4) → scores -0.3, -0.6. From “The”: top-2 are “cat” (-0.2), “dog” (-0.3) → scores -0.5, -0.6. Keep top-2 overall: [("I love", -0.3), ("The cat", -0.5)].
  • Continue until <eos>.

Computational cost. Per step: \(B\) forward passes (one per beam), or one batched forward of size \(B\). Memory: \(B\) KV caches. Total: \(\sim B\) × greedy cost.

Common assessment traps.

  • Beam search is not sampling — it is deterministic given the model.
  • Greedy decoding equals beam search with \(B=1\).
  • Beam search maximizes likelihood, but maximum-likelihood text is often poor quality for open-ended generation (Holtzman et al.).
  • Diverse beam search is a structural change (groups + penalty), not a hyperparameter.

9.2 Sampling Techniques

Why sample at all? For open-ended generation (chat, story-writing, creative tasks), the maximum-likelihood sequence is often bland or repetitive. Sampling introduces randomness, producing diverse outputs that match the LLM’s learned distribution.

The basic operation. At each step, draw the next token from a distribution derived from the model’s logits. The exact distribution is controlled by temperature and various truncation schemes.

(A) Temperature.

Apply \(T > 0\) to the logits before softmax:

\[ p_i(T) = \frac{\exp(z_i / T)}{\sum_j \exp(z_j / T)} \]

  • \(T = 1\): model’s natural distribution.
  • \(T < 1\): sharpens — high-probability tokens get more probability, low ones less. The limit \(T \to 0\) is greedy.
  • \(T > 1\): flattens — increases diversity but risks low-quality samples.

Typical chat models use \(T = 0.7\)–\(1.0\). Code models often use \(T = 0.2\)–\(0.4\) for deterministic-ish output.

(B) Top-k sampling. Fan et al., 2018, “Hierarchical Neural Story Generation.”

Keep only the top \(k\) logits; zero out the rest; renormalize; sample.

def top_k(logits, k):
    v, _ = torch.topk(logits, k)
    logits = logits.clone()
    logits[logits < v[..., [-1]]] = -float("inf")
    return logits
  • \(k = 1\): greedy.
  • \(k = 50\): typical chat default.
  • Large \(k\): closer to unrestricted sampling.

Problem. Fixed \(k\) ignores the shape of the distribution. If the model is confident (one option much higher than all others), top-\(k\) with large \(k\) admits noise. If the model is uncertain (uniform-ish), top-\(k\) with small \(k\) chops off plausible options.

(C) Top-p (nucleus) sampling. Holtzman et al., 2019, “The Curious Case of Neural Text Degeneration.”

Adaptive: keep the smallest set of tokens whose cumulative probability exceeds \(p\).

def top_p(logits, p):
    sorted_logits, sorted_idx = logits.sort(descending=True)
    cum = sorted_logits.softmax(-1).cumsum(-1)
    keep = cum < p
    keep[..., 1:] = keep[..., :-1].clone()
    keep[..., 0]  = True                              # always keep top-1
    mask = keep.scatter(-1, sorted_idx, keep)
    return logits.masked_fill(~mask, -float("inf"))
  • \(p = 0.95\): typical chat default.
  • \(p = 0.9\): tighter, more focused.
  • \(p = 1.0\): no truncation.

Top-\(p\) adapts: in confident distributions only a few tokens make the cut; in flat distributions many do.

(D) Combining temperature, top-k, top-p. Order matters: temperature, then top-k truncation, then top-p truncation, then sample. HuggingFace’s generate() does exactly this.

(E) Min-p sampling. Nguyen et al., 2024.

Keep tokens whose probability exceeds \(\min_p \cdot p_\max\), where \(p_\max\) is the most-probable token. Adaptive like top-p but scales with the maximum probability — more robust at high temperatures.

threshold = min_p * max(probs)
keep = probs >= threshold

Typical \(\min_p \in [0.05, 0.1]\).

(F) Typical sampling. Meister et al., 2022, “Locally Typical Sampling.”

Sample from the set of tokens whose log-probability is near the conditional entropy of the distribution (rather than the highest log-probs). Motivation: human text uses tokens of “typical” information content, not always the most likely ones. Hyperparameter \(\tau \in (0, 1)\) — the probability mass to retain.

(G) Repetition penalty. Keskar et al., 2019, “CTRL.”

Divide the logit of any token that has appeared in the generated context (within a window) by \(r > 1\):

\[ z_i \leftarrow z_i / r \quad \text{if } i \in \text{generated} \]

(For negative logits, the formula is \(z_i \cdot r\) — i.e., scale logits toward zero either way.)

Common \(r \in [1.1, 1.3]\). Reduces but doesn’t eliminate repetition. The newer frequency penalty (subtract \(\alpha \cdot \text{count}(i)\)) and presence penalty (subtract \(\beta\) if seen once) refine the idea.

(H) Contrastive decoding. Li et al., 2022, “Contrastive Decoding.”

Compare a large expert model with a small amateur model; reward tokens the expert finds plausible but the amateur does not:

\[ \text{score}(y) = \log p_\text{expert}(y \mid x) - \log p_\text{amateur}(y \mid x) \]

(with constraints to keep \(p_\text{expert}\) above a threshold). Pushes the output toward what is distinctively “good model” behavior. Best for analytical / factual writing; less for creative tasks.

(I) Contrastive search. Su et al., 2022.

Combine model probability with a penalty for similarity to previously generated tokens. At each step, candidate token \(v\) is scored:

\[ \text{score}(v) = (1-\alpha) p(v|x,y_{<t}) - \alpha \max_{j < t} \text{sim}(h_v, h_{y_j}) \]

Reduces degeneration without the harshness of repetition penalties.

(J) DRY sampling. “Don’t Repeat Yourself” — recent (2024) repetition-suppression scheme that penalizes any token starting a previously-seen sequence of length \(\geq 2\).

Worked example — temperature math. Suppose logits are \(z = (2, 1, 0)\).

  • \(T=1\): \(p \propto (e^2, e^1, e^0) = (7.39, 2.72, 1.00)\); normalize → \((0.665, 0.244, 0.090)\).
  • \(T=0.5\): \(z/T = (4, 2, 0)\); \(p \propto (54.6, 7.39, 1)\) → \((0.867, 0.117, 0.016)\).
  • \(T=2.0\): \(z/T = (1, 0.5, 0)\); \(p \propto (2.72, 1.65, 1.00)\) → \((0.506, 0.307, 0.187)\).

Smaller \(T\) sharpens, larger \(T\) flattens — exactly as expected.

Common assessment traps.

  • Top-k vs top-p. Top-k truncates by count; top-p truncates by cumulative probability. Top-p is adaptive; top-k is not.
  • Temperature applies to logits before softmax. Many implementations apply it before truncation.
  • Beam search ≠ “sampling with beams”; beam search is deterministic.
  • Greedy is just argmax; it has no temperature, no top-p.
  • Repetition penalty is applied to logits, not to probabilities — it works for both positive and negative logits via division by \(r\).

MODULE 10 — Designing Prompt-Based Behaviors

10.1 Zero-Shot and Few-Shot Prompting for Instruction Following

Definition and motivation. A prompt is the input text the LLM is conditioned on. The art and engineering of prompting is choosing inputs that elicit the desired behavior without modifying the model’s weights.

  • Zero-shot prompt. No examples; just an instruction and the task input.
  • Few-shot prompt (in-context learning, ICL; Brown et al., 2020). The prompt contains \(k\) examples of the task before the actual query. The model infers the pattern from the examples.

Zero-shot.

Translate to French: "I love language models."

Few-shot.

Translate to French.

English: "Hello, how are you?"
French:  "Bonjour, comment allez-vous?"

English: "The weather is nice."
French:  "Le temps est agréable."

English: "I love language models."

Why few-shot works. Brown et al. (2020, GPT-3) showed that as model scale grew, in-context learning emerged: the model behaves as if it had been fine-tuned on the examples in its prompt, despite no weight updates. The mechanism is not fully understood (induction heads / Bayesian view both contribute) but empirically robust.

Prompt-engineering principles (cumulative).

  1. Be specific. Vague instructions yield vague outputs.
  2. Specify the format. “Reply as a JSON object with fields name, age.” LLMs often follow format directives literally.
  3. Decompose. Long, multi-part tasks benefit from numbered sub-instructions.
  4. Provide a persona. “You are a senior code reviewer…” — sets style.
  5. Show, don’t tell. Few-shot examples often outperform verbose explanations.
  6. Constrain. “Answer in at most two sentences.” “Do not use the word X.”
  7. Ground. Provide context. Retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) is a prompt technique scaled to corpora.
  8. Plan, then act. Asking the model to “think step-by-step” (CoT, Section 10.2) before answering improves complex reasoning.
  9. Use delimiters. Triple backticks, XML-like tags (<context>...</context>), or fenced examples help the model recognize structural boundaries.
  10. Specify the output schema. Most modern LLMs support structured output (JSON mode, grammar-constrained decoding).

System prompts. Many chat-tuned LLMs accept a system role with model-wide instructions. It’s a strong knob for persona and behavior shaping, but can be overridden by sufficiently emphatic user text (prompt injection risk).

Selecting few-shot examples. Several heuristics, ranked by effort:

  • Random examples from a training set — works, but suboptimal.
  • Semantic-similarity retrieval. Embed the query, retrieve the \(k\) most similar examples. Often a meaningful improvement.
  • Diversity-aware selection. Combine similarity with diversity to avoid redundant examples.
  • Class-balanced selection (for classification).
  • Adaptive / data-curated few-shot — pre-curate per-cluster exemplars; pick the cluster matching the query.

Number of shots. Diminishing returns past 3–10 examples for most tasks. Very long contexts may admit 50+ examples (“many-shot ICL”; Agarwal et al., 2024).

Example sensitivity. Example order, prompt phrasing, and even punctuation can move accuracy by several points (Lu et al., 2022, “Fantastically Ordered Prompts”). Best practice: sweep small variations on a held-out set; don’t claim performance from a single prompt.

Common assessment traps.

  • In-context learning is not training. Weights do not change.
  • Few-shot is a form of prompting, not fine-tuning.
  • Instruction tuning enables zero-shot performance; without it the base model often needs few-shot examples to behave.
  • Prompts are part of the contract — when you switch model versions, re-test your prompts.

10.2 Chaining Prompts and Reasoning Strategies

A single prompt can do only so much. Modern LLM applications chain prompts and tool calls into pipelines: each step’s output feeds the next; verifiers gate progress; tools are invoked. This section covers the canonical chaining and reasoning strategies.

(A) Chain-of-Thought (CoT). Wei et al., 2022, “Chain-of-Thought Prompting Elicits Reasoning in Large Language Models.”

Ask the model to show its reasoning before answering. Two variants:

  • Few-shot CoT. Include examples with explicit reasoning chains.
Q: Alice has 5 apples. She gives 2 to Bob. How many does she have?
A: Alice starts with 5 apples. She gives 2 away. 5 - 2 = 3. The answer is 3.

Q: Tom has $20. He spends $7. How much is left?
A:
  • Zero-shot CoT (Kojima et al., 2022). Append “Let’s think step by step.” to the prompt; the model produces a reasoning chain even without examples.

CoT typically improves performance on arithmetic, commonsense, and symbolic reasoning tasks by 10–40 points at the 100B+ scale. Smaller models benefit less or not at all (“emergent” capability).

(B) Self-Consistency. Wang et al., 2022, “Self-Consistency Improves Chain of Thought Reasoning in Language Models.”

Sample \(N\) CoT chains (temperature > 0), extract each chain’s final answer, majority-vote. The intuition: many reasoning paths can lead to the same correct answer; few wrong answers are reached by many independent paths.

chains = [model.generate(prompt, temperature=0.7) for _ in range(40)]
answers = [extract_answer(c) for c in chains]
final   = mode(answers)

Lifts GSM8K accuracy by 10–20 points on top of CoT. Costs \(N\times\) inference.

(C) Tree of Thoughts (ToT). Yao et al., 2023, “Tree of Thoughts: Deliberate Problem Solving with Large Language Models.”

Generalize CoT into a tree of partial solutions; at each step the LLM proposes several next steps; a verifier (LLM-as-judge or programmatic) scores them; expand the most promising branches; backtrack when needed.

Three components:

  1. Thought decomposition. Split the task into discrete reasoning steps.
  2. Thought generator. Propose \(k\) candidate continuations per state.
  3. State evaluator. Score states (“sure / maybe / impossible”).
  4. Search algorithm. BFS, DFS, or beam over the tree.

Effective on combinatorial puzzles (Game of 24), creative writing with constraints, crosswords. Much more compute per query than CoT.

(D) ReAct (Reason + Act). Yao et al., 2022, “ReAct: Synergizing Reasoning and Acting in Language Models.”

Interleaves reasoning (think) and acting (tool-call) tokens in a single trace:

Thought: I should look up the population of Hyderabad.
Action: search("population of Hyderabad")
Observation: Hyderabad has approximately 10 million people.
Thought: The question asks for the population in 2010. Let me search more specifically.
Action: search("Hyderabad population 2010")
Observation: 7.75 million in 2010.
Thought: Now I have the answer.
Answer: 7.75 million.

Standard pattern for tool-using agents. The model emits an action; the orchestrator parses it, runs the tool, returns an observation; the model continues.

(E) Reflexion. Shinn et al., 2023, “Reflexion: Language Agents with Verbal Reinforcement Learning.”

After a failed attempt, the agent generates a verbal critique (a “reflection”) and retries. The critique is stored in a memory buffer; subsequent attempts see prior critiques. Effectively self-improvement at inference time, without weight updates. Works well for code generation (use unit-test failures as the signal) and HotpotQA.

(F) Program of Thoughts (PoT) / PAL. Chen et al., 2022 (PoT); Gao et al., 2022 (PAL).

Instead of doing arithmetic / logical reasoning in natural language, the model writes a Python program that, when executed, produces the answer. The program’s output is the answer.

Q: If a train travels 60 mph for 3.5 hours, how far does it go?

Program:
speed = 60
time = 3.5
distance = speed * time
print(distance)

Output: 210

Offloads arithmetic to the Python interpreter, where errors are impossible.

(G) Self-Refine. Madaan et al., 2023, “Self-Refine: Iterative Refinement with Self-Feedback.”

A loop: generate → critique → refine. The same LLM plays generator, critic, and refiner. Iterate until the critique says “good” or budget is exhausted. Useful for writing tasks.

(H) Plan-and-Solve. Wang et al., 2023.

Two-stage: ask the model to write a high-level plan, then execute the plan step-by-step. Encourages decomposition before reasoning.

(I) Function calling / Tool use. Modern LLMs (GPT-4, Claude, Gemini, Llama 3.1) are trained to emit structured function calls when given a tool schema:

[
  {"name": "search", "description": "Web search", "parameters": {"q": {"type": "string"}}},
  {"name": "calc",   "description": "Eval math",  "parameters": {"expr": {"type": "string"}}}
]

The model decides whether to call a tool; emits a JSON object naming the function and arguments; the runtime executes; the result is fed back as a tool-response message; the model continues.

Both ReAct and modern structured tool calling describe the same idea — structured tool calling is the more reliable, schema-enforced form.

(J) Prompt chaining (a.k.a. pipelines).

A chain is a directed graph of LLM calls and tool calls. Each node consumes outputs of upstream nodes and produces inputs for downstream nodes.

Q --> [classify intent] --> route
                              ├── (research)
                              │   └── [search] -> [summarize] -> [answer]
                              ├── (compute)
                              │   └── [program] -> [execute] -> [answer]
                              └── (chat)
                                  └── [answer]

Frameworks: LangChain, LlamaIndex, Haystack, DSPy (Stanford NLP — programmatic + auto-optimizing prompts).

(K) Agents. A loop of LLM calls + tools + memory + (sometimes) planning. The LLM decides what to do next based on the goal, prior actions, and observations. The recent surge in agentic systems (AutoGPT, BabyAGI, OpenAI Assistants, Anthropic computer-use, OpenAI o1’s deliberative reasoning) builds on the techniques above.

(L) Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG). Covered exhaustively in the companion RAG study guide; here it is “use a retriever to fetch context before the LLM call.” The most widely deployed prompt-chaining pattern.

Common assessment traps.

  • CoT is a prompt; it is not a model. Any sufficiently capable LLM can be CoT-prompted.
  • Self-Consistency operates on CoT samples — it requires temperature > 0.
  • ToT is a search procedure, not a single prompt.
  • ReAct interleaves reasoning and tool calls in one trace; tool calling (the OpenAI/Anthropic spec) is a structured form of the same idea.
  • Reflexion adds verbal self-critique to memory between attempts; it does not update weights.

MODULE 11 — Measuring Language Model Performance

11.1 Task-Specific Evaluation Metrics

This subsection enumerates the metrics named in the syllabus (perplexity, BLEU, ROUGE, METEOR, BERTScore, exact match, F1, pass@k, MMLU, GSM8K, HumanEval, MT-Bench, Arena Elo, judge-LLM evals) and the standards-of-practice around using them.

(A) Perplexity.

The exponential of the cross-entropy. Measures how surprised a language model is by held-out text.

\[ \operatorname{PPL}(\mathbf{x}) = \exp\!\Big(-\frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^N \log p_\theta(x_i \mid x_{<i})\Big) = \exp(\mathrm{H}) \]

where \(\mathrm{H}\) is the average cross-entropy per token in nats. With natural log, the formula is \(\exp\); with log base 2, it is \(2^H\) — both are perplexity, just in different “units.”

Properties.

  • \(\operatorname{PPL} \geq 1\). A perfect model has \(\operatorname{PPL} = 1\); a uniform model over \(V\) tokens has \(\operatorname{PPL} = V\).
  • Tokenizer-dependent: a model with a smaller vocabulary produces more tokens per word and tends to have lower perplexity per token. Cross-tokenizer comparisons are misleading.
  • Length-normalized: dividing by \(N\) removes the document-length bias.

Worked numeric example. A model assigns the held-out sentence “The cat sat” probabilities \(p_1=0.5, p_2=0.4, p_3=0.6\). Cross-entropy \(= -(\ln 0.5 + \ln 0.4 + \ln 0.6)/3 \approx 0.671\). \(\operatorname{PPL} = e^{0.671} \approx 1.96\).

(B) BLEU (Bilingual Evaluation Understudy). Papineni et al., 2002.

Measures n-gram overlap between a generated text and one or more reference translations.

  1. For each \(n\) (typically \(n=1,\dots,4\)), compute the clipped modified \(n\)-gram precision \(p_n\): fraction of generated \(n\)-grams that appear in the reference, clipped per \(n\)-gram to the maximum count in the reference.
  2. Geometric-mean precisions: \(\prod_n p_n^{w_n}\), with \(w_n = 1/4\) typically.
  3. Brevity penalty to discourage too-short outputs:

\[ \text{BP} = \begin{cases} 1 & c > r \\ e^{1 - r/c} & c \le r \end{cases} \]

where \(c\) = candidate length, \(r\) = reference length.

  1. BLEU \(= \text{BP} \cdot \exp\!\sum_n w_n \log p_n\).

Range: \([0, 1]\) (often reported as \([0, 100]\)). Higher is better.

Drawbacks. Precision-only; ignores synonyms; rewards literal phrases; uncorrelated with quality on creative tasks.

(C) ROUGE. Lin, 2004.

Recall-oriented metrics for summarization. Variants:

  • ROUGE-N — \(n\)-gram recall against references.
  • ROUGE-L — longest-common-subsequence (LCS) based F-measure.
  • ROUGE-S — skip-bigram.

For summarization, ROUGE-1 (unigram recall) and ROUGE-2 (bigram recall) are reported alongside ROUGE-L.

(D) METEOR. Banerjee & Lavie, 2005.

Includes stemming and synonym matching (via WordNet). Weighted F-mean of precision and recall, with a fragmentation penalty for word-order. Better correlation with human judgments than BLEU for short outputs.

(E) BERTScore. Zhang et al., 2019.

Embed each candidate and reference token with a contextual encoder (BERT, RoBERTa); compute cosine similarities; greedy alignment; report precision, recall, F1 over aligned similarities. Captures semantic similarity that BLEU misses.

(F) Exact Match (EM). A binary metric: did the model’s output exactly equal the gold answer? Used in SQuAD-style QA (after normalizing whitespace, punctuation, articles).

(G) F1 (token-overlap). For span-prediction QA: the F1 of the predicted token set against the gold token set. SQuAD reports both EM and F1.

(H) pass@k (code). Chen et al., 2021, “Evaluating Large Language Models Trained on Code” (Codex paper).

Probability that at least one of \(k\) generated samples passes the unit tests. Estimated unbiasedly via:

\[ \text{pass}@k = \mathbb{E}_\text{problems}\!\left[1 - \binom{n - c}{k} / \binom{n}{k}\right] \]

where \(n\) is samples generated, \(c\) is the number that pass, \(k\) is the budget. Commonly reported at \(k = 1, 10, 100\).

(I) MMLU. Hendrycks et al., 2020, “Measuring Massive Multitask Language Understanding.”

57 subjects (math, physics, law, medicine, history, …), 4-way multiple choice, ~14k questions. Tests broad knowledge. Reported as overall accuracy and per-subject accuracy.

(J) GSM8K. Cobbe et al., 2021, “Training Verifiers to Solve Math Word Problems.”

8.5k grade-school math word problems with integer answers and detailed solutions. Standard benchmark for arithmetic reasoning. Reported as accuracy (exact-match on the final integer).

(K) HumanEval. Chen et al., 2021.

164 hand-written Python coding problems with unit tests. Reported as pass@k.

(L) MT-Bench. Zheng et al., 2023, “Judging LLM-as-a-Judge.”

80 multi-turn chat queries spanning 8 categories. Each response scored 1–10 by an LLM judge (GPT-4). Reports mean score; sometimes per-category breakdown.

(M) Chatbot Arena Elo. Zheng et al., 2023; lmsys.org.

Crowdsourced pairwise preference: users blind-test two anonymous chatbots; the winning model accumulates Elo points. Reflects head-to-head human preference rather than any single benchmark.

(N) LLM-as-judge. Use a strong LLM (GPT-4, Claude) to grade responses with a rubric. Cheaper than human labels; biased (verbosity bias, position bias, self-preference). Mitigations: random position, calibration prompts, multiple judges.

Position bias. LLM judges prefer the first option in pairwise comparison. Mitigation: present each pair twice in both orders and average.

Verbosity bias. Judges prefer longer responses regardless of quality.

Self-bias. A judge LLM prefers responses generated by the same family. Use a different family as the judge when possible.

Common assessment traps.

  • Perplexity is tokenizer-dependent — cannot compare across tokenizers.
  • BLEU = precision; ROUGE = recall; both ignore semantics.
  • BERTScore captures semantics; correlated with human judgments.
  • pass@1 and pass@k are different settings of the same metric.
  • MT-Bench uses GPT-4 as judge; results are not the same as Elo (human preference).
  • Decontamination of training data is required before reporting MMLU / GSM8K / HumanEval — otherwise the model has memorized the test set.

11.2 Zero-Shot Evaluation

Definition. Zero-shot evaluation measures how well a model performs a task without task-specific fine-tuning and without in-context examples. Just the instruction (or, for log-likelihood evaluation, no instruction at all).

Two formulations.

  1. Generative zero-shot. Prompt the model with the question; sample a response; score (exact match, regex, BLEU, judge).
Q: What is the capital of France?
A:
  1. Log-likelihood zero-shot. For multiple-choice tasks, compute the log-probability the model assigns to each candidate answer (with the same prefix), pick the argmax.
P(A | Q): "The capital of France is Paris."   → log p_a
P(B | Q): "The capital of France is London."  → log p_b
...
choose argmax

Log-likelihood evaluation is deterministic and tokenization-stable, but only applies to fixed-option tasks. Generative evaluation is more realistic but introduces decoding variance.

Lengt normalization. For LL evaluation, longer candidates have lower joint probability. Common fix: divide log-prob by the number of tokens (or characters, or bytes). EleutherAI’s lm-evaluation-harness exposes acc (raw) and acc_norm (length-normalized) for many tasks.

Why zero-shot matters.

  • Measures the model’s generalization to unseen task formats.
  • Allows fair comparison: every model is evaluated on the same prompt.
  • Required for reasoning about emergent capabilities — abilities that appear without explicit training.

Tooling. The de facto standard is EleutherAI’s lm-evaluation-harness. Supports MMLU, ARC, HellaSwag, TruthfulQA, GSM8K (CoT), HumanEval, and hundreds more, with consistent log-likelihood and generative scoring.

lm_eval --model hf \
        --model_args pretrained=meta-llama/Meta-Llama-3-8B \
        --tasks mmlu,arc_challenge,hellaswag,gsm8k \
        --num_fewshot 0 \
        --batch_size 8

Few-shot harness. Same setup but with \(k\) in-context examples drawn from the dataset’s dev split. Few-shot accuracy is usually 2–10 points higher than zero-shot.

Common assessment traps.

  • Zero-shot evaluation does not necessarily prompt the model to “answer the question.” With base models, an empty zero-shot prompt may continue rather than answer. Use log-likelihood evaluation for base models on multiple-choice tasks; use instruction-tuned models for generative zero-shot.
  • Decontamination is critical. Any model that has seen the test set will score artificially high.
  • Zero-shot ≠ unsupervised — the model was trained on a vast supervised-ish corpus; “zero-shot” refers only to the task presentation.

MODULE 12 — Visualizing and Explaining Behavior

12.1 Visualizing Attention Weights

Definition and motivation. Attention weights \(A \in \mathbb{R}^{L\times L}\) assign each query position a probability distribution over key positions. Visualizing them tells you which tokens each token attended to — a coarse but informative view of how the model is routing information.

Tools.

  • BertViz (Vig, 2019, “A Multiscale Visualization of Attention in the Transformer Model”). Browser-based, supports HuggingFace models. Three views:
    • Head view: attention from each token to each other token, per head, per layer.
    • Model view: small-multiples grid of all heads across all layers.
    • Neuron view: dot-product attention with Q/K vectors shown.
  • exBERT (Hoover et al., 2019). Browser app for BERT-family models with cluster analysis of attention patterns.
  • TransformerLens / nnsight / Pyvene (more advanced; for mechanistic interpretability).

Reading an attention map.

  • Diagonal-dominant pattern: token attends mostly to itself.
  • Off-diagonal stripes: token attends to specific relative positions.
  • “Look-back” heads: attend to specific previous occurrences of the same token (induction heads).
  • “Global” heads: attend to special tokens like [CLS] or BOS.

Caveats.

  • A high attention weight is not a causal explanation. Attention \(\neq\) saliency. Two papers (Jain & Wallace 2019; Wiegreffe & Pinter 2019) debated whether attention is “explanation”; consensus: attention shows where information can flow, not where it must.
  • Multi-head attention diffuses interpretation; one head’s pattern may be canceled by another.
  • Visualization helps generate hypotheses; rigorous attribution requires causal interventions (Module 12.2).

Code — visualizing attention in HuggingFace.

from transformers import AutoTokenizer, AutoModel

attentions = out.attentions     # tuple of (B, n_heads, L, L) per layer

import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
attn_l0_h0 = attentions[0][0, 0].detach().numpy()
plt.imshow(attn_l0_h0, cmap="viridis")
plt.xticks(range(len(inputs.input_ids[0])), tok.convert_ids_to_tokens(inputs.input_ids[0]), rotation=90)
plt.yticks(range(len(inputs.input_ids[0])), tok.convert_ids_to_tokens(inputs.input_ids[0]))

12.2 Interpreting Attention Maps and Mechanistic Interpretability

This subsection goes beyond visualization into the mechanistic interpretability program: reverse-engineering what specific components of a transformer do.

The residual-stream view. Elhage et al. (2021), “A Mathematical Framework for Transformer Circuits.” Treat the transformer as components that read from and write to a \(d\)-dimensional residual stream. Each attention head and MLP is a function read(residual) -> write(residual). Interpretability ≈ identifying which directions each component reads and writes.

(A) Induction heads. Olsson et al. (2022), “In-context Learning and Induction Heads.”

A specific two-attention-head circuit that implements pattern-matching in-context: given a sequence ... A B ... A, the model predicts B. The first head identifies the previous occurrence of token A; the second copies the token that followed it.

Induction heads are present in essentially every transformer LLM beyond a few hundred parameters and explain a substantial fraction of in-context learning. They emerge during training at a specific compute threshold (“phase transition”).

(B) Circuits. Olah, Cammarata et al. (2020, “Zoom In: An Introduction to Circuits”) and the transformer-circuits.pub series. A circuit is a sub-graph of model components (heads, MLPs, neurons) jointly implementing a behavior — e.g., indirect-object identification (Wang et al. 2022).

Methods to discover circuits:

  • Activation patching. Replace one component’s activation with a different prompt’s activation; see how outputs change. Identifies which components contribute causally.
  • Path patching. Trace specific routes through the graph.
  • Ablation. Zero out or mean-ablate components; measure performance drop.

(C) Probing classifiers. Conneau et al. (2018); Tenney et al. (2019).

Train a linear (or shallow MLP) probe on top of frozen model activations to predict a linguistic property (POS tag, dependency relation, named-entity boundary, factual claim). If the probe scores high, the property is linearly decodable from those activations.

Caveat: a high-accuracy probe shows the information is present, not that the model uses it. Causal probes (Elazar et al., 2021 “amnesic probing”) test usage by removing the information and remeasuring.

(D) Logit lens. nostalgebraist (2020 blog post).

Project each layer’s residual-stream output through the final LM head to inspect the next-token distribution as if generation stopped at that layer. Reveals how predictions evolve across depth — early layers often have shallow guesses; later layers refine.

final_norm = model.transformer.ln_f
lm_head    = model.lm_head
for i, h in enumerate(hidden_states):       # one per layer
    logits = lm_head(final_norm(h))
    print(i, top_k(logits[:, -1]))

(E) Tuned lens. Belrose et al., 2023, “Eliciting Latent Predictions from Transformers with the Tuned Lens.”

Improves logit lens by training a small affine probe per layer to better project residual streams into logit space. Smoother, more informative depth-curves of predictions.

(F) Sparse Autoencoders (SAEs). Cunningham et al., 2023, “Sparse Autoencoders Find Highly Interpretable Features in Language Models”; Anthropic’s “Scaling Monosemanticity” (2024).

Train an autoencoder on a transformer layer’s activations with an L1 sparsity penalty:

\[ h \to \text{encoder} \to z \quad \text{(sparse code)} \quad \to \text{decoder} \to \hat{h} \]

Loss: \(\|h - \hat{h}\|^2 + \lambda \|z\|_1\).

The dictionary \(z\) has many more features than \(h\)’s dimension. Each \(z\) feature, when interpreted, often corresponds to a monosemantic concept (“the Golden Gate Bridge”, “Python list comprehensions”, “anger about traffic”). Anthropic’s 2024 work scaled SAEs to frontier models, recovering tens of millions of features.

SAEs are now a primary tool for circuit-finding, feature steering (“amplify this feature, suppress that one”), and safety auditing.

(G) Activation steering / representation engineering. Turner et al., 2023; Zou et al., 2023, “Representation Engineering.”

Add a learned (or hand-crafted) direction to the residual stream during inference to bias the model toward a behavior (truthfulness, helpfulness, refusal). Cheap and effective for some behaviors; less reliable than fine-tuning.

(H) Causal tracing. Meng et al., 2022, “Locating and Editing Factual Associations in GPT” (ROME). Identify which layer / token positions store a specific fact by patching activations from a corrupted run into a clean run.

Tools for interpretability.

Common assessment traps.

  • Attention weights are not explanations — they show where information could flow, not which information actually shaped the output.
  • Probing shows decodability, not causal use.
  • Logit lens projects intermediate residual streams through the final unembedding; tuned lens learns a per-layer projection.
  • Sparse autoencoders find features, not full circuits; circuits are wired-together features.
  • Mechanistic interpretability is the program of reverse-engineering transformers, distinct from post-hoc explainability (SHAP, saliency).

APPENDIX C — Self-Test Bank

50 questions covering the syllabus. Each is followed by a concise answer plus a pointer to the section with the full treatment.

Part 1 — Data

Q1. Order the stages of a pretraining data pipeline: deduplication, language ID, quality filtering, text extraction, decontamination. A. text extraction → language ID → quality filtering → deduplication → decontamination (then PII/toxicity → mixing → tokenization). §1.1.

Q2. What does MinHash + LSH accomplish in a data pipeline? A. Near-duplicate (fuzzy) deduplication at corpus scale. Documents whose Jaccard similarity over shingles exceeds a threshold are pruned. §1.1.

Q3. Why is decontamination important? A. Removes any document overlapping the evaluation benchmarks; without it, evaluation scores are inflated by memorization. §1.1.

Q4. Match the license to the training-use status: (a) CC0, (b) CC-BY-NC, (c) GPL, (d) All-rights-reserved with robots opt-out. A. (a) freely usable; (b) non-commercial only; (c) usable but viral / debated for derivative obligations; (d) exclude. §1.2.

Q5. What is the EU’s TDM exception? A. A copyright exception in the EU Digital Single Market Directive that allows text and data mining unless rightsholders explicitly opt out. §1.2.

Q6. Why might Books3 be excluded from modern training corpora? A. It is a corpus of pirated books; legal exposure forced its retraction; modern open mixes (RedPajama, Dolma) exclude it. §1.2.

Q7. Distinguish “vision encoder” and “projector” in a multimodal LLM. A. The vision encoder (large, e.g., ViT-L) produces image features; the projector (small MLP or Q-Former) maps those features into the LLM’s embedding dimension and (optionally) compresses sequence length. §2.1.

Q8. What is Self-Instruct? A. A method to bootstrap an instruction dataset from a small seed: prompt an LLM with seed examples; ask it to generate new instructions; have it produce inputs and outputs; filter; iterate. §2.2.

Q9. Name three risks of training on synthetic data. A. Mode collapse (low effective diversity), hallucination amplification, distribution drift / bias inheritance from the generator. §2.2.

Q10. Compare BPE and WordPiece’s merge criteria. A. BPE merges by raw co-occurrence frequency; WordPiece merges by a likelihood score (≈ pointwise mutual information): \(\text{freq}(ab)/(\text{freq}(a)\cdot\text{freq}(b))\). §2.3.

Q11. Why does byte-level BPE not need a <UNK> token? A. Its initial vocabulary contains every byte, so any Unicode string is representable as a sequence of bytes. §2.3.

Q12. What is the role of ▁ in SentencePiece? A. It represents whitespace as a normal character within tokens — making the tokenizer language-independent. §2.3.

Q13. Why is o200k_base cheaper per character than cl100k_base? A. Larger vocabulary → more merges → fewer tokens per character. The trade-off is a bigger embedding/output-head footprint. §2.3.

Part 2 — Architecture

Q14. Derive the output shape of scaled dot-product attention given \(X \in \mathbb{R}^{B\times L\times d}\). A. \(Y \in \mathbb{R}^{B\times L\times d_v}\) with \(d_v = d\) in typical settings. The attention matrix is \((B, L, L)\). §3.1.

Q15. Why \(\sqrt{d_k}\) in the scaling? A. Without it, dot products grow with \(d_k\); softmax saturates and gradients vanish. Scaling keeps variance ~1. §3.1.

Q16. Compute attention with \(Q = K = V = I_2\) and verify the row sums. A. As in §3.1: \(A \approx \begin{pmatrix}0.67 & 0.33\\0.33 & 0.67\end{pmatrix}\), each row sums to 1. §3.1.

Q17. What is the KV cache and what does it save? A. At inference, cache the \(K, V\) tensors of every previously generated token so each new token requires only one new \(K, V\) row. Reduces per-token cost from \(O(L^2 d)\) to \(O(L d)\). §3.1.

Q18. Why does GQA save KV-cache memory vs MHA? A. Heads in a group share a single \(K, V\). If there are \(g\) groups and \(h\) heads, the cache shrinks by \(h/g\). §3.2.

Q19. What is FlashAttention and is it exact? A. A tiled, fused-softmax implementation of exact scaled dot-product attention. Same math; never materializes the \(L\times L\) matrix in HBM; ~2–10× faster. Exact. §3.2.

Q20. What is RoPE applied to? A. The query and key vectors — rotation by an angle proportional to position. Encodes relative position into the dot product. §3.2.

Q21. Pre-norm vs post-norm. A. Pre-norm: \(y = x + \text{Sublayer}(\text{LN}(x))\). Post-norm: \(y = \text{LN}(x + \text{Sublayer}(x))\). Pre-norm is more stable at scale. §4.1.

Q22. Why use RMSNorm over LayerNorm in modern LLMs? A. Fewer FLOPs (no mean subtraction, no bias) with equivalent training stability. §4.1.

Q23. What activation does SwiGLU use, and how many input projections does it have? A. Swish (\(x\cdot\sigma(x)\)) inside; gated against a second input projection. Two input projections (\(W_1, W_3\)) + one output (\(W_2\)). §4.1.

Q24. What is weight tying, and what does it save? A. The LM head shares weights with the token embedding (\(W_\text{lm}=E^\top\)). Saves \(V \cdot d\) parameters and often improves generalization. §4.2.

Q25. What is the residual stream? A. A conceptual \(d\)-dimensional bus that each transformer block reads from and writes to; foundational for mechanistic interpretability. §4.2.

Part 3 — Adapting

Q26. Distinguish SFT, CPT, instruction tuning. A. CPT: continued pretraining on next-token loss over domain text. SFT: supervised fine-tuning on (input, output) pairs, loss masked to output. Instruction tuning: a type of SFT where pairs are (instruction, response). §5.1.

Q27. What is LoRA’s parameterization? A. \(\Delta W = BA\) with \(A \in \mathbb{R}^{r\times d_\text{in}}\), \(B \in \mathbb{R}^{d_\text{out}\times r}\), \(r \ll \min(d_\text{in}, d_\text{out})\). \(A\) Kaiming-init, \(B\) zero-init. §5.2.

Q28. What are the three QLoRA innovations? A. (1) 4-bit NormalFloat (NF4); (2) double quantization (quantize the scales); (3) paged optimizer states via unified memory. §5.2.

Q29. Match: BitFit, prompt tuning, IA3, DoRA, prefix tuning. A. BitFit — train only biases. Prompt tuning — learn input-embedding soft prompt. IA3 — learned scalars on K, V, FFN. DoRA — magnitude + direction LoRA. Prefix tuning — learnable K, V prefixes at every layer. §5.2.

Q30. Order the three stages of RLHF. A. SFT → Reward Modeling → RL fine-tuning (PPO). §6.1.

Q31. Why is there a KL penalty in PPO RLHF? A. To keep the policy near the reference (SFT) model and prevent reward hacking by drifting into regions the RM scores high but humans dislike. §6.1.

Q32. What does DPO eliminate from the RLHF pipeline? A. The explicit reward model and the on-policy RL loop. Trains directly on preference pairs with a closed-form loss involving a reference model. §6.1.

Q33. Compare KTO and DPO data requirements. A. DPO needs paired (chosen, rejected) preferences. KTO needs unpaired desirable / undesirable labels. §6.1.

Q34. What is Constitutional AI? A. Anthropic’s recipe: write a constitution → self-critique + revise outputs (SFT stage) → AI-judged preference labels → RL or DPO. §6.1.

Q35. Name three forms of transfer learning with pretrained LLMs. A. Fine-tuning, in-context learning, PEFT (LoRA), distillation, prompt engineering. §6.2.

Part 4 — Efficiency

Q36. Compare PTQ and QAT. A. PTQ: quantize after training using calibration data; cheap; may lose accuracy at 4-bit. QAT: insert fake-quantize ops in the forward pass during training; more expensive; recovers accuracy at low bits. §7.1.

Q37. Name the key insight of GPTQ. A. Quantize columns of a weight matrix one at a time using a Hessian-based correction to remaining columns; reduces perplexity gap vs naive PTQ. §7.1.

Q38. What does AWQ “see” about activations? A. The magnitude distribution of activations entering each weight column; weights multiplying large activations are protected from aggressive quantization by per-channel scaling. §7.1.

Q39. What is W8A8 (as in SmoothQuant)? A. Both weights and activations quantized to int8. Requires “smoothing” activation outliers into weights to be stable. §7.1.

Q40. Knowledge distillation loss components. A. (1) KL between student and teacher softened logits, scaled by \(T^2\); (2) standard cross-entropy on hard labels; often (3) feature/attention alignment. §7.2.

Q41. Why does BF16 not need loss scaling? A. It has the same 8-bit exponent as FP32, so gradients don’t underflow into denormals the way FP16’s 5-bit exponent allows. §8.1.

Q42. Order the ZeRO stages by what they shard. A. ZeRO-1: optimizer states. ZeRO-2: + gradients. ZeRO-3: + parameters (≡ FSDP-FullShard). §8.2.

Q43. Tensor parallel vs pipeline parallel. A. TP splits matrices within a layer across GPUs (in-node, NVLink). PP splits layers across GPUs with micro-batched pipelining (across nodes). §8.2.

Q44. What problem does paged attention solve? A. KV-cache memory fragmentation and inefficient reservation. Blocks of KV cache are virtualized via a block table; enables higher batch sizes and prefix sharing. §8.2.

Q45. Is speculative decoding lossy? A. No. The verifier model’s output distribution is exactly preserved; only the wall-clock time changes. §8.2.

Part 5 — Output

Q46. Top-k vs top-p sampling. A. Top-k: keep the \(k\) highest-prob tokens regardless of distribution shape. Top-p: keep the smallest set whose cumulative prob ≥ \(p\) — adaptive. §9.2.

Q47. What is self-consistency and what does it require? A. Sample \(N\) CoT reasoning chains and majority-vote their answers. Requires \(T > 0\) to get diverse samples. §10.2.

Q48. Compare ToT and ReAct. A. ToT searches a tree of partial reasoning steps with branch evaluation. ReAct interleaves reasoning and tool actions in a single linear trace. §10.2.

Part 6 — Evaluation & Interpretability

Q49. Perplexity formula and units. A. \(\operatorname{PPL} = \exp(-\frac{1}{N}\sum \log p(x_i|x_{<i}))\); dimensionless; depends on tokenizer. Lower is better; bounded below by 1. §11.1.

Q50. What does logit lens show? A. The next-token distribution implied by an intermediate layer’s residual stream, projected through the final unembedding. Reveals how predictions evolve with depth. §12.2.
